## SECTION 1: TOWN LAYOUT AND ORGANIC GROWTH PATTERNS

### 1.1 How Medieval Towns Actually Grew

Medieval towns were **never grid-based** (with rare exceptions like bastide towns in southern France). They grew organically through accretion over decades or centuries.

**Growth Pattern:**
1. A nucleus forms: a crossroads, a river ford, a church, or a lord's castle gate
2. The **market square** becomes the functional center (often irregular, not rectangular)
3. Roads radiate outward from the market, following terrain contours and desire lines
4. **Burgage plots** (long narrow lots, ~5-7m wide x 30-60m deep) line the main streets, maximizing street frontage for commerce
5. Back lanes develop behind the burgage plots for service access
6. Secondary streets branch off main roads at irregular angles
7. Building density is highest at center, decreasing toward walls
8. Narrow alleys (1-2m wide) thread between buildings, often covered by upper-story overhangs
9. Town walls are built when the settlement is prosperous enough, enclosing the built-up area with limited gates (typically 2-4)

**Key Dimensions:**
- Market square: 30x50m to 80x120m (irregular shapes common)
- Main streets: 4-8m wide
- Secondary streets: 2-4m wide
- Alleys: 0.8-2m wide
- Burgage plot width: 5-7m (street frontage)
- Burgage plot depth: 30-60m (extending back from street)
- Town wall circuit: 500-2000m for small towns, up to 5km for major cities

### 1.2 Town Zones (Center Outward)

#### Market Square / Town Center
- Irregular open space, often triangular or trapezoidal
- Town hall (Rathaus) on one side
- Market cross or market hall in center
- Pillory / stocks for public punishment
- Main well or fountain
- Cobblestone paving (rest of town is packed earth)
- Wealthy merchant houses face the square (stone ground floors, timber upper stories)
- 2-4 stories tall, with shop fronts at street level

#### Merchant Quarter
- Surrounds the market square
- Stone-built ground floors (fire protection for valuable goods)
- Timber-framed upper stories with jettied (overhanging) construction
- Warehouses and counting rooms behind shops
- Best-maintained streets, possibly cobbled
- Guild halls for major merchant guilds

#### Artisan Quarter
- Grouped by trade (street names survive: Tanner Row, Smith Street, Baker Lane)
- Noisier/dirtier trades pushed further from center
- Blacksmiths near the gate (fire risk, need for traveler service)
- Potters near clay sources
- Weavers cluster together (shared water for fulling)
- Open workshops facing the street
- Living quarters above or behind workshop
- Smoke, noise, smell characterize this quarter

#### Residential Quarter
- Townhouses of middling citizens
- Timber-framed construction, wattle-and-daub infill
- 2-3 stories, narrow frontage (4-6m), deep plan
- Small rear gardens / yards
- Shared privies (cesspits) accessed from back lanes

#### Religious Quarter
- Parish church (stone, the largest building after any castle)
- Churchyard / graveyard surrounding it
- Possibly a monastery or friary with cloister
- Almshouses for the poor
- Church school
- Often on slightly elevated ground

#### Slums / Poor Quarter
- Against the town walls (worst land, damp, shadowed)
- Near the gates (noise, traffic, danger)
- Smallest, most densely packed buildings
- Single-story hovels, often shared
- Open sewers, refuse pits
- Closest to tanneries and other noxious trades

#### Outside the Walls
- Suburbs develop around gates along main roads
- Tanneries (need water, produce terrible stench -- always outside)
- Leper houses / lazarettos (isolated)
- Gallows and execution grounds
- Lime kilns, brick kilns
- Cemeteries for outsiders
- Farms and market gardens begin immediately

### 1.3 Infrastructure

#### Water Supply
- **Town wells**: stone-lined shafts, 5-20m deep, with wooden or stone wellhead and bucket-and-rope or windlass mechanism
- **Public fountains**: fed by gravity from higher ground via wooden or lead pipes (Augsburg had a sophisticated piped system)
- **River access**: steps or ramps down to water for washing, animals, and industrial use
- **Rain barrels**: at every building, collecting from roof gutters
- Castle wells could be extremely deep: Ronneburg (96m), Kyffhausen (167m), Regenstein (196m)

#### Drainage and Sanitation
- **Central gutter**: streets typically had an open gutter running down the center or along one side
- **Cesspits**: 1-2m deep, stone-lined, behind houses -- emptied by "gong farmers" periodically
- **Privies**: wooden seats over cesspits, or projecting from upper stories (garderobes)
- Cordoba (10th-13th century) had a centrally-planned underground sewer network -- exceptional for its era
- Most European towns relied on surface drainage until the 18th-19th century
- Streets sloped toward the river or a drainage ditch

#### Market Crosses and Pillories
- **Market cross**: stone cross on stepped base, marks the legal market area (1.5-3m tall)
- **Pillory**: wooden post with arm holes, or stocks (seated restraint), near market cross
- **Butter cross**: smaller covered structure for dairy/perishable sales

#### Town Gates
- 2-4 gates in the wall circuit, aligned with main roads
- Gate tower: 8-12m tall, passage 3-4m wide, 4-6m deep
- Wooden double doors, iron-banded
- Portcullis in larger towns
- Tax collection point: all goods entering pay tolls
- Gates closed at night (curfew bell signals closing)
- Gate guards (2-4 men) check entrants during the day

#### Bridge Crossings
- Stone bridges with shops built on them (like old London Bridge)
- Wooden bridges for smaller crossings
- Ford crossings marked with posts
- Bridge chapel (small shrine for travelers)
- Toll collection at bridge entry

## SECTION 2: FARMS AND AGRICULTURAL LAND

### 2.1 The Open Field System

Medieval farming used the **three-field system** surrounding the town:

**Layout:**
- Three large fields (100-300 acres each), divided into narrow strips
- Each strip (selion): ~200m long x 5-10m wide (1 furlong x 1 chain is the classic measure: 201m x 20m)
- Strips separated by unploughed "balks" (grass boundaries, ~0.5m wide)
- Each peasant family held strips scattered across all three fields (ensuring fair distribution of good/bad land)
- One field fallow each year (rotated: wheat, then barley/oats, then fallow)

**Supporting Land:**
- **Common land**: shared grazing for all villagers' animals (often on poorer ground, hilltops, or marshes)
- **Meadow**: riverside grassland for hay-making (highly valued, often divided into strips like arable)
- **Woodland**: managed coppice for fuel, building timber, and pannage (pig grazing on acorns)
- **Waste**: uncultivated scrubland at the edges of the manor

**Distances:**
- Fields typically begin 50-200m from the town walls
- Open fields extend 1-3km from the settlement
- A manor's total agricultural land: 500-2000 acres
- Peasant walk to furthest strips: up to 30 minutes

### 2.2 Farm Buildings

#### Farmhouse (Longhouse / Byre-Dwelling)
- **Plan**: rectangular, 12-18m long x 5-7m wide
- **Division**: family living space at one end, animal byre at the other, separated by a cross-passage
- **Construction**: stone footings (0.6-1m high), timber-frame or cruck frame above, thatched roof
- **Interior**: central hearth (no chimney in early period), beaten earth floor, smoke hole in roof
- **Byre end**: cobbled floor for drainage, stalls for 4-8 cattle
- The "Black Forest house" and "Frisian Gulf house" are regional variants of this byre-dwelling type

#### Barn (Tithe Barn / Aisled Barn)
- **Plan**: 15-30m long x 7-10m wide, three-aisled with large central nave
- **Construction**: timber frame with stone or timber walls, thatched or tiled roof
- **Key feature**: large entrance doors on one or both long sides (3-4m wide, 4-5m tall) for loaded wagons
- **Interior**: central threshing floor (hard-packed earth or flagstone) between the doors; hay/grain stored in bays (mows) on either side
- **Tithe barns**: built by monasteries to store the tenth (tithe) of local produce; often the grandest agricultural buildings
- 12th-century building tradition; thousands existed across Western Europe by the 15th century

#### Dovecote (Columbarium)
- **Shape**: circular or square tower, 3-6m diameter, 4-8m tall
- **Roof**: pyramidal (square) or conical (round), with cupola or louver for bird entry
- **Interior**: walls lined with nesting boxes (pigeonholes), arranged in rows from floor to ceiling
- **Access**: a rotating ladder (potence) on a central post allows reaching all nesting boxes
- **Capacity**: 200-1000+ nesting boxes in large examples
- **Privilege**: keeping pigeons was often a **manorial right** -- only the lord could have a dovecote
- Provides: meat, eggs, and valuable dung (fertilizer)

#### Root Cellar / Ice House
- **Root cellar**: dug into a hillside or mound, stone-lined, earth-covered; 2-3m wide x 3-5m deep
- **Ice house**: egg-shaped or cylindrical underground chamber; packed with ice cut from ponds in winter
- Both maintain cool, stable temperature (~4-8C) year-round
- Thick walls (0.5-1m) of stone or brick with earth packing

#### Beehives
- **Skep type**: woven straw dome, 30-40cm diameter, placed on stone or wooden platforms
- Arranged in rows in a sheltered, south-facing location (bee garden / bee bole)
- **Bee boles**: recesses built into a wall (stone niches 40x40x40cm) to shelter skeps from weather
- 3-12 hives per farmstead typical

#### Other Farm Structures
- **Granary**: raised on staddle stones (mushroom-shaped stones, ~0.6m tall) to prevent rat access; timber frame, 3x4m
- **Cart shed**: open-fronted timber structure for wagons
- **Hay rick**: stacked hay on a raised platform, thatched cap
- **Pond**: for watering livestock, fish farming, and retting flax (2-5m diameter)
- **Kitchen garden**: fenced plot near the farmhouse (5x10m to 10x20m), herbs and vegetables
- **Orchard**: near a water source, 10-30 fruit trees (apple, pear, plum, cherry)

## SECTION 3: CASTLE ARCHITECTURE AND TERRAIN

### 3.1 Real Castles on Unique Terrain

#### Hilltop Castles

**Krak des Chevaliers (Syria)** -- 12th century, Knights Hospitaller
- Built on a 650m-high hill between Homs and the Mediterranean
- **Concentric design**: two massive walls separated by a moat cut into rock
- Inner wall rises above outer wall for overlapping fields of fire
- Glacis (sloped stone apron) at base prevents mining
- Layout dictated by hilltop shape: elongated oval, ~200m x 140m
- Considered the finest surviving Crusader castle

**Edinburgh Castle (Scotland)** -- 12th century onward
- Built on Castle Rock, a volcanic plug with sheer cliffs on three sides
- Only approachable from the east (long ramp approach)
- Buildings cut into rock and connected by terraces at different levels
- Layout is irregular, conforming to the rock's shape
- The "Lang Stairs" connect levels internally

#### Cliff Castles

**Dunnottar Castle (Scotland)** -- 14th century keep, 13th-17th century complex
- Perched on a 50m (160ft) sea cliff headland
- Surrounded on three sides by the North Sea
- Only accessible via a narrow, steep path down and up a natural gap
- Site covers ~1.4 hectares (3.5 acres)
- Buildings include: keep, gatehouse, chapel, L-plan palace, stables, brewery, smithy
- Natural defense is so strong that minimal wall construction was needed on sea-facing sides

**Predjama Castle (Slovenia)** -- 12th century origin
- Built into the mouth of a 123m-high cliff cave
- Cave provides: natural walls, water supply, ventilation, and a secret escape tunnel to the cliff top
- Castle rooms incorporate natural cave formations as walls and ceilings
- Nearly invisible from a distance -- only visible at close approach
- Secret vertical shaft through rock allows resupply even during siege
- 404-foot cliff face serves as the back wall

#### Island / Water Castles

**Bodiam Castle (England)** -- 1385
- Symmetrical rectangular plan: 4 corner towers, 1 gate tower, 4 intermediate towers
- Completely surrounded by a wide water-filled moat (originally landscaped)
- Approach via a right-angled causeway (forces attackers to expose flanks)
- Inner dimensions: ~48m x 48m; wall height: ~18m
- The moat is both defensive and ornamental

**Eilean Donan Castle (Scotland)** -- 13th century origin
- Built on a small island where three sea lochs meet (Loch Duich, Loch Long, Loch Alsh)
- Originally a large curtain-wall castle enclosing most of the island
- Accessed only by boat or a narrow causeway (modern bridge added later)
- Strategic position controlling the sea approach to the Scottish Highlands
- Built to defend against Viking raids

#### Mountain Pass Castles

**Marksburg Castle (Germany)** -- 13th century
- Perched on a steep 150m hill above the Rhine River
- Controls a narrow stretch of the Rhine valley (natural chokepoint)
- The only hilltop castle on the Rhine never destroyed
- Compact layout conforming to the narrow hilltop
- Multiple terraces and baileys at different elevations

#### River Bend Castles

**Conwy Castle (Wales)** -- 1283-1292, Edward I / Master James of St George
- Built on a rock promontory at the mouth of the River Conwy
- Protected by the river on the south and east
- 8 massive round towers, each ~21m (70ft) tall
- Two wards: outer and inner, divided by a cross-wall
- Integrated with the town walls (1.3km circuit with 21 towers and 3 gates)
- Castle dimensions: ~73m x 18m (elongated to fit the rocky ridge)

**Caernarfon Castle (Wales)** -- 1283-1330, Edward I
- Sits between the River Seiont and the Menai Strait
- Plan dictated by terrain, incorporating an earlier Norman motte
- Polygonal towers (unique, inspired by Constantinople's walls)
- Banded masonry: light limestone with dark sandstone stripes
- Eagle Tower: largest tower, ~9m diameter, with three turrets
- Town walls extend from the castle, enclosing the entire planned town

### 3.2 How Terrain Shapes Castle Plans

| Terrain | Shape | Key Adaptation |
|---------|-------|----------------|
| Hilltop (rounded) | Roughly circular / oval | Walls follow contour of hilltop |
| Hilltop (elongated ridge) | Long and narrow | Linear sequence of wards (Conwy) |
| Cliff edge | Irregular, open on cliff side | Minimal walls on cliff face, heavy defense on approach |
| Island | Rectangular or fills island | Moat = water body, causeway is only approach |
| Mountain spur | Triangular, narrowing to point | Keep at narrow end (strongest point), gate at wide end |
| River bend | D-shaped or follows riverbank | River serves as moat on 1-3 sides |
| Cave mouth | Irregular, built into rock | Cave provides roof, walls, and escape routes |

### 3.3 Castle Layout and Zones

#### Outer Ward (Outer Bailey)
- First area entered after the main gate
- **Buildings**: stables (10-20 horses), smithy/forge, barracks (20-100 soldiers), kitchen (separate building due to fire risk), bakehouse, brewhouse, well
- **Character**: working area, noisy, smoky, animals present
- **Dimensions**: 30-80m across, depending on castle size
- Packed earth ground with cobblestones near buildings

#### Inner Ward (Inner Bailey)
- Accessed through a secondary gate from the outer ward
- **Buildings**: great hall (the castle's main room, 15-25m x 8-12m), chapel, lord's chambers (solar), buttery, pantry, garderobe towers
- **Character**: cleaner, more prestigious, restricted access
- **The Great Hall**: single largest room in the castle
  - Raised dais at one end for the lord's table
  - Central hearth or wall fireplace
  - High windows with shutters (glass rare and expensive)
  - Timber roof with hammer-beam or king-post trusses
  - Dimensions: 15-25m long x 8-12m wide x 6-10m to roof ridge

#### Keep / Donjon
- **Function**: last line of defense, treasury, prison (basement), lord's private quarters
- **Shape**: square (Norman, 12th c.) or round/D-shaped (13th c. onward)
- **Dimensions**: 10-20m per side (square) or 10-15m diameter (round); 20-30m tall
- **Walls**: 2.5-4m thick at base, tapering slightly upward
- **Floors**: 3-5 levels connected by spiral stairs (clockwise ascending, favoring right-handed defenders)
- **Ground floor**: storage, well access, sometimes prison
- **Upper floors**: hall, chambers, chapel
- **Roof**: crenellated parapet with corner turrets
- Had its own well, ensuring water supply even if the rest of the castle fell
- Entrance often at first-floor level via removable stairs or drawbridge

#### Curtain Walls
- **Thickness**: 2.5-6m (8-20ft), with rubble-filled core between dressed stone faces
- **Height**: 9-13m (30-44ft) typical
- **Wall walk (chemin de ronde)**: 1.2-1.8m wide path along the top, protected by crenellations
- **Crenellations**: alternating merlons (solid, ~1.5m wide x 1m tall) and embrasures/crenels (gaps, ~0.5m wide)
- **Machicolations**: stone brackets projecting from wall top with floor gaps for dropping stones/hot liquids; replaced wooden hoardings from 13th c. onward
- **Arrow loops**: vertical slits (10-15cm wide x 60-120cm tall) in the wall; cross-shaped variants allow horizontal aiming. Wall splays inward to give archer a wider field of fire inside

#### Towers
- **Corner towers**: positioned at wall corners for maximum flanking fire; round or D-shaped
- **Flanking towers**: along wall segments every 30-50m (within bow range of each other)
- **Gate towers**: paired towers flanking the main gate, often the most heavily fortified
- **Tower dimensions**: 6-10m diameter (round) or 6-8m per side (square); 15-25m tall
- **Internal**: 3-5 floors with arrow loops at each level; spiral stair
- Project forward from the wall face to allow enfilade fire along the wall base

## SECTION 4: CASTLE WALLS AND GATES

### 4.1 Wall Construction Details

#### Wall Cross-Section (Outside to Inside)
1. **Batter / talus**: sloped stone base (45-60 degrees), 1-2m tall -- deflects dropped objects outward, resists mining
2. **Dressed stone outer face**: cut stone blocks, mortared joints
3. **Rubble core**: loose stone and morite fill
4. **Dressed stone inner face**: cut stone blocks
5. **Wall walk**: 1.2-1.8m wide paved path
6. **Parapet (outer side)**: crenellated wall, ~1m tall on the merlon, ~0.5m on embrasure sill
7. **Inner parapet or no wall**: sometimes an inner low wall to prevent falling

#### Wall Dimensions Reference
| Component | Dimension |
|-----------|-----------|
| Total wall thickness | 2.5-6m (small castle: 2.5m; major fortress: 4-6m) |
| Wall height (base to wall walk) | 8-12m typical; up to 15m for major fortresses |
| Parapet height (merlons) | 1.5-2m above wall walk |
| Merlon width | 1.2-1.8m |
| Embrasure (crenel) width | 0.4-0.6m |
| Wall walk width | 1.2-1.8m |
| Batter/talus height | 1-2m |

#### Arrow Loops (Embrasures)
- **Basic slit**: 8-15cm wide exterior, 60-120cm tall; interior splay widens to 60-100cm
- **Cross loop (cruciform)**: vertical slit + horizontal slit intersection; allows horizontal aiming
- **Fishtail loop**: slit with a widened base (inverted V), allows downward aiming
- **Stirrup loop**: rounded base for crossbow
- **Gun loop (15th c.)**: round hole below a slit, for early firearms
- Wall thickness at loop: full wall thickness; the loop is a tapered slit through the mass

#### Machicolations
- **Construction**: stone corbels (brackets) project 0.5-1m from the wall face at parapet level
- **Floor**: stone slabs laid across corbels with gaps (20-30cm) between them
- **Purpose**: drop stones, hot sand, quicklime, boiling water through the floor gaps
- Replaced wooden **hoardings** (temporary timber galleries hung from wall top) from the late 13th century
- Continuous machicolations along the wall top are a hallmark of late medieval castles

#### Wall Segments for Procedural Generation
- **Straight segment**: 5-15m length between towers (modular, tileable)
- **Corner segment**: 90-degree turn (can be a tower or a wall bend)
- **T-junction**: wall meets perpendicular wall (often at a tower)
- **Gate segment**: wall interrupted by gate passage (3-4m wide, 4-6m deep)
- **Wall-to-tower joint**: wall butts into a tower; the tower projects forward ~3m from the wall line
- **Stepped segment**: where wall changes elevation on sloped terrain

### 4.2 Gate Types and Mechanisms

#### Simple Wooden Gates
- Double doors, each leaf 1.5-2m wide x 3-4m tall
- Oak planks, 10-15cm thick, reinforced with iron bands (horizontal straps every 30-50cm)
- Iron studs (nail heads, 5-8cm diameter) across the face
- **Hinges**: massive iron strap hinges, 60-100cm long, mounted on iron pintles set in stone
- **Bar**: heavy timber drawbar slides through iron brackets into a socket in the wall (oak beam, 10-15cm square)
- **Lock**: iron lock mechanism, operable from inside only

#### Portcullis
- **Construction**: heavy timber grid (oak beams 15-20cm square) with iron sheathing or entirely wrought iron
- **Dimensions**: 2.5-4m wide x 3-5m tall, weight 500-2000kg
- **Guide grooves**: vertical channels (15-20cm deep x 15-20cm wide) cut into the stone jambs on each side
- **Operating mechanism**: located in the room directly above the gate passage
  - Winch drum with ratchet and pawl (prevents uncontrolled dropping)
  - Heavy chains (or ropes in earlier examples) attached to the top crossbar
  - 2-4 men required to raise; drops by gravity when released
- **Bottom edge**: iron-shod points (spikes) that seat into slots in the threshold
- **Multiple portcullises**: major castles had 2 or 3 in sequence, creating a **kill zone** between them
  - Space between portcullises: 3-6m (enough to trap a group of attackers)
  - Murder holes in the ceiling between the portcullises

**For animation (separate objects):**
- The portcullis grid: **single rigid body**, translates vertically in guide grooves
- The winch drum: **rotates** as chain pays out or reels in
- Chains: **linked chain** connecting portcullis top to winch drum

#### Drawbridge
- **Dimensions**: 3-5m wide x 4-8m long (spans the moat or ditch)
- **Construction**: heavy timber platform (oak planks, 10-15cm thick, on timber frame)
- **Hinge point**: at the castle-side threshold (iron pins in stone)

**Mechanism types:**

1. **Counterweight (bascule) drawbridge**:
   - Bridge pivots on an axle at the castle wall
   - Rear section (inside the gate passage) is weighted
   - When rear drops, front rises -- relatively easy to operate
   - Counterweight chamber visible inside the gatehouse

2. **Chain-and-winch drawbridge**:
   - Chains attached to the outer edge of the bridge
   - Chains run up through slots in the gatehouse wall to a winch above
   - Winch drum with capstan handles; 4-6 men to raise

3. **Turning bridge (pivot bridge)**:
   - Pivots on a central axle (like a seesaw)
   - One half spans the moat, other half extends into the gate passage
   - Rare; used at some later medieval castles

**For animation (separate objects):**
- Bridge deck: **single rigid body**, rotates around hinge axis at castle wall base
- Chains (x2): deform/extend as bridge lowers, retract as bridge raises
- Winch wheels: **rotate** synchronized with chain movement
- Counterweight (if bascule type): translates vertically, opposite to bridge motion

#### Barbican
- Extended gate defense projecting outward from the main gate
- Creates a narrow, walled passage (2-4m wide x 10-30m long) leading to the actual gate
- Walls lined with arrow loops; murder holes in ceiling
- Often has its own gate or portcullis at the outer end
- Forces attackers through a killing corridor under fire from three sides
- Examples: Warwick Castle barbican, Carcassonne barbican

#### Murder Holes
- Openings in the ceiling of the gate passage (20-40cm square or round)
- Arranged in a line or grid pattern
- Used for: dropping stones, pouring boiling water, hot sand, quicklime
- Also used to pour water to extinguish fires set against the gate
- Located between portcullises for maximum effect

### 4.3 Gate Passage Cross-Section (Outside to Inside)
1. Drawbridge (if moat present)
2. Outer portcullis
3. Murder holes in ceiling (zone 1)
4. Kill zone (3-6m long)
5. Inner portcullis
6. Murder holes in ceiling (zone 2)
7. Double wooden doors (main gate)
8. Drawbar sockets in wall

## SECTION 5: DOORS

### 5.1 Medieval Door Types

#### Plank Door (Simplest)
- **Construction**: vertical oak planks (15-25cm wide x 3-5cm thick), butted edge-to-edge
- **Fastening**: horizontal battens (cross-pieces) nailed or pegged on the back, typically 3
- **Use**: common houses, outbuildings, storage rooms
- **Dimensions**: 0.8-1.2m wide x 1.6-2.0m tall
- **Material**: oak, elm, or locally available hardwood

#### Ledged and Braced Door
- **Construction**: vertical planks with horizontal ledges (top, middle, bottom) AND diagonal braces forming a Z-pattern on the back
- **Advantage**: the diagonal bracing prevents sagging on the hinge side
- **Use**: better-quality houses, workshops, barns
- **Dimensions**: 0.9-1.4m wide x 1.8-2.2m tall

#### Double-Plank Door (Strongest Common Type)
- **Construction**: two layers of planks -- vertical on the exterior face, horizontal (or diagonal) on the interior face -- nailed through with clinched iron nails
- **Advantage**: extremely strong, resists splitting in any direction
- **Use**: castle internal doors, gatehouses, important buildings
- **Dimensions**: 0.9-1.5m wide x 1.8-2.5m tall

#### Studded Door
- **Construction**: plank door with iron nail heads (studs) driven into the exterior face in a decorative/protective pattern
- **Stud size**: dome-headed iron nails, 3-8cm diameter heads
- **Pattern**: grid, diamond, or decorative arrangements
- **Purpose**: prevents axes from biting into the wood; the nail heads deflect blade edges
- **Use**: castle doors, wealthy townhouses, guild halls
- **Dimensions**: 1.0-1.8m wide x 2.0-3.0m tall

#### Church Door
- **Shape**: pointed arch (Gothic) or round arch (Romanesque)
- **Construction**: heavy oak planks, often with decorative ironwork covering most of the face
- **Iron strap hinges**: massive, extending 60-100cm across the door, often with decorative scrollwork, fleur-de-lis terminals, or dragon motifs
- **Ring handle**: iron ring (15-25cm diameter) mounted on a decorative plate, serves as both handle and door knocker
- **Lock**: iron plate with keyhole, mounted at waist height
- **Decoration**: carved tympanum above the door (stone relief); iron scroll-and-leaf strapping
- **Dimensions**: 1.2-2.5m wide x 2.5-4.0m tall (double doors for major churches: 3-4m total width)

#### Dungeon / Prison Door
- **Construction**: heaviest oak planks (5-8cm thick), reinforced with iron bands across the full face
- **Window**: small barred opening (15x20cm) at eye height, with iron grille
- **Lock**: heavy bolt mechanism, operable from outside only
- **Hinges**: concealed or protected on the outside to prevent tampering
- **Use**: castle dungeons, prison cells, treasury doors
- **Dimensions**: 0.7-1.0m wide x 1.5-1.8m tall (deliberately small to force ducking)

#### Trapdoor
- **Construction**: heavy planks with iron ring handle (flush-mounted)
- **Hinges**: iron strap hinges along one edge
- **Use**: cellar access, roof access, oubliette covers
- **Dimensions**: 0.6-0.9m square or 0.6x0.9m rectangular
- **Lock**: bolt or padlock hasp on the upper side

#### Secret / Hidden Door
- **Rotating wall panel**: section of stone wall on a central pivot, balanced to swing with a push
- **Bookcase door**: wooden shelving unit on hidden hinges, released by a latch behind a specific book or carving
- **Fireplace passage**: entrance concealed behind a fireplace back panel
- **Triggered**: pressure plate in floor, specific stone that pushes in, carved detail that rotates
- **Critical**: must look identical to surrounding wall/furniture when closed

### 5.2 Door Construction for 3D Generation

#### Component Breakdown (What to Model Separately)

**Door Frame (static, part of wall)**
- Stone surround: 15-25cm deep reveal cut into the wall
- Timber frame (for timber buildings): 10-15cm square posts and lintel
- Threshold stone: flat stone at floor level, slightly raised (2-5cm)
- The frame is part of the wall mesh or a separate insert piece

**Door Leaf (animated, MUST be separate mesh)**
- The swinging panel -- this is the object that rotates on its hinge axis
- Pivot point: at the hinge edge, at the vertical center line of the hinge pins
- For double doors: TWO separate meshes, each with its own pivot at its outer edge
- For trapdoors: pivot at the hinge edge (one side)
- For portcullises: pivot point irrelevant (translate up/down instead)

**Hinges (can be part of door mesh or separate for close-up detail)**
- **Strap hinges**: iron straps, 60-100cm long x 3-5cm wide x 0.5cm thick
  - Decorative end shapes: arrow point, fleur-de-lis, leaf, coiled scroll
  - Mounted with large iron nails through the strap into the door planks
  - Loop or eye at the hinge end, hung on an iron pintle (pin) in the frame
- **Pin hinges**: simple iron pin-and-sleeve, hidden between door edge and frame
- Typically 2-3 hinges per door (top, bottom, and optionally middle for heavy doors)

**Handle / Ring / Latch (separate mesh for interaction)**
- **Ring handle**: iron ring (12-20cm diameter) hung from a decorative backplate (8-15cm)
- **Latch**: iron lever that lifts to release, with thumb press
- **Bar**: interior timber bar resting in iron brackets (for barricading)

**Lock Plate and Keyhole (surface detail or separate mesh)**
- Iron plate (8-12cm x 5-8cm) with keyhole cutout
- Mounted at 90-110cm height from ground
- Key: large iron key, 10-20cm long, with ornate bow (handle)

#### Procedural vs. AI-Generated (Tripo) Decision Guide

| Component | Method | Reason |
|-----------|--------|--------|
| Plank door (common) | **Procedural** | Simple geometry: planks + battens + studs |
| Door frame (stone) | **Procedural** | Architectural element, modular, consistent scale |
| Iron strap hinges | **Procedural** | Simple flat geometry with decorative end |
| Ring handle | **Procedural** | Torus + backplate, simple geometry |
| Lock plate | **Procedural** | Flat plate with cutout |
| Church door (ornate) | **Tripo** | Complex carved reliefs, organic decorative iron |
| Secret door mechanism | **Procedural** | Must integrate precisely with wall mesh |
| Dungeon door (weathered) | **Procedural + texture** | Geometry is simple; visual detail comes from texture |
| Portcullis grid | **Procedural** | Regular lattice pattern, must fit guide grooves exactly |
| Drawbridge deck | **Procedural** | Flat plank platform, must match moat span exactly |

### 5.3 Animation Pivot Points Reference

| Door Type | Pivot Location | Motion |
|-----------|---------------|--------|
