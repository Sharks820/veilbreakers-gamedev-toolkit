## 1.1 Peasant Cottage

### Purpose
Home to a serf, tenant farmer, or common laborer and their family (4-8 people). The entire household lives, cooks, eats, sleeps, and often keeps small livestock in a single main room. The building doubles as workspace for spinning, mending tools, and processing food.

### Exterior Appearance
- **Walls:** Lime-washed wattle-and-daub panels between exposed dark oak timber framing (cruck frame construction). The daub is a mixture of clay, straw, cow dung, and sand, applied over a woven hazel-wattle lattice. Over time, the lime wash yellows and flakes, revealing grey-brown daub beneath. Cracks spider-web across the surface where the daub has dried and shrunk.
- **Roof:** Steeply pitched thatch (wheat straw or reed) reaching nearly to the ground on the sides. Ridge is capped with a turf or woven straw ridge piece. A smoke hole or louvered vent at the ridge apex lets smoke escape. Moss and lichen colonize the north face. The thatch darkens from golden to grey-brown with age.
- **Foundation:** Rough fieldstone or flint rubble base course, about 18 inches high, to keep the wattle-and-daub off damp ground. Unmortared or crudely lime-mortared.
- **Openings:** One single-plank oak door (4 feet high, requiring stooping) with hand-forged iron strap hinges and a wooden latch. One or two small window openings (about 18 inches square) closed with hinged wooden shutters -- no glass. In better cottages, oiled linen or scraped animal parchment stretched across the window lets in faint diffuse light.
- **Size:** Roughly 15-20 feet long by 10-12 feet wide. Sometimes a lean-to extension for animal housing (a byre).
- **Surrounding:** A wattle fence encloses a small kitchen garden (cabbages, leeks, onions, beans). A midden heap sits downwind.

### Interior -- Single Main Room

**Floor:** Packed earth, worn smooth and slightly concave from centuries of foot traffic. Strewn with rushes, dried herbs (meadowsweet, lavender, tansy), and occasionally bracken fern to absorb moisture and mask odor. The rushes are changed seasonally; the lowest layers rot and blacken.

**Central Hearth:** An open fire pit lined with flat river stones, positioned slightly off-center. No chimney -- smoke rises to the thatch above, blackening the underside of the roof timbers to a deep tar-brown. An iron chain or adjustable pot hook (a ratchet trammel) hangs from a roof beam above the hearth. From this dangles:
- A cast-iron or copper cauldron (the household's most valuable item) for pottage
- A smaller iron pot or skillet for warming milk or frying
- A trivet (three-legged iron stand) sits in the embers for smaller pots

**Cooking/Food Area (near hearth):**
- A wooden salt box nailed to the wall near the fire (salt is precious)
- A bread paddle (peel) leaning against the wall
- A wooden bread trough for kneading dough
- A hand quern (two flat stones) for grinding grain, set on a low wooden platform
- Hanging bundles of dried herbs (thyme, rosemary, sage) from the roof beams
- A few clay pots and wooden bowls stacked on a crude shelf
- A leather water bucket and wooden ladle
- An iron flesh-hook for retrieving meat from the pot

**Dining:**
- A rough-hewn oak trestle table (planks on two X-shaped legs) that can be dismantled and leaned against the wall to save space
- One or two benches (backless planks on legs)
- Wooden trenchers (flat boards used as plates) and a shared wooden drinking cup or leather jack

**Sleeping:**
- Straw-stuffed palliasses (mattresses) laid directly on the earthen floor, rolled up during the day and stacked against the wall
- A single rough woolen blanket per person, perhaps a sheepskin for warmth
- In slightly better cottages, a low wooden bedframe with rope supports for the master and wife; children sleep on the floor
- A wooden cradle for an infant, carved from a single log or rough planks

**Storage:**
- A single large oak chest (the family's "strongbox") for storing clothing, documents, valuables
- Wooden pegs driven into the wall beams for hanging clothing, a cape, tools
- A wicker basket or two for eggs, vegetables
- Bags of grain (oats, barley) hung from beams to keep from rats
- A barrel for salted meat or fish

**Tools/Work:**
- A drop spindle and distaff (for spinning wool) hung on the wall
- A hand sickle, a hoe, and a wooden rake leaning by the door
- A whetstone on the window ledge
- A tallow candle holder (simple iron spike) -- candles are expensive, rarely used

**Livestock (if byre-house):**
- The far end of the cottage may be separated by a low wattle partition, housing 1-2 cows, a few chickens, or a pig during winter. Their body heat helps warm the dwelling.

### Textures and Colors
- **Walls:** Cream-to-dirty-yellow lime wash over grey-brown daub; dark brown-to-black oak timbers, weathered and split
- **Roof interior:** Soot-blackened thatch and beams; the higher the soot layer, the more waterproof the thatch becomes
- **Floor:** Dark brown packed earth with pale green-gold rushes
- **Furniture:** Unfinished oak and ash, silvered with age; knife-marks and burn marks visible
- **Metals:** Blackened iron (pot hooks, hinges), dull copper-green patina on the cauldron
- **Textiles:** Undyed wool in natural cream/brown/grey; linen in pale flaxen color

### Lighting
- **Primary:** The open hearth fire -- warm orange-amber, flickering, with a haze of smoke above eye level
- **Secondary:** Faint grey daylight through the oiled-linen window or open shutters
- **Rare:** A tallow rush-light (a rush dipped in rendered fat, held in an iron clip) -- smoky, dim, yellow light. Beeswax candles only for the wealthiest peasants or special occasions
- **Night:** Near total darkness except hearth embers

### Atmosphere/Smell Cues
- Wood smoke is the dominant scent -- a permanent, acrid, slightly sweet smell that permeates everything
- Damp earth and mildew from the floor rushes
- Cooking smells: pottage (boiled grain, onions, root vegetables), rendered animal fat
- Animal odor if livestock share the space (cow manure, straw, wet hide)
- Dried herbs provide a faint herbal note cutting through the heavier smells
- In winter: close, humid, oppressive warmth from bodies and animals; condensation on cold walls

## 1.2 Townhouse (Merchant/Artisan)

### Purpose
Urban dwelling for a middle-class merchant, craftsman, or skilled artisan. The ground floor serves as a shop or workshop open to the street; upper floors are living quarters. Houses are narrow-fronted (12-20 feet) but deep (40-60 feet), reflecting the high cost of street frontage in medieval towns.

### Exterior Appearance
- **Ground floor:** Rubble stone or dressed limestone walls, 2 feet thick, providing security for goods. A wide shop front with a hinged wooden counter-shutter that folds down to create a display shelf during business hours and folds up to close the shop at night. One solid oak door to the side leads to a passage to the upper floors and rear yard.
- **Upper floors (1-2 above):** Jettied (overhanging) timber-frame construction with wattle-and-daub or plaster infill panels. Each upper story projects 1-2 feet beyond the one below, supported on carved oak bracket corbels. The timber framing is exposed and painted dark brown or black; infill panels are lime-plastered and may be painted white, ochre, or pale blue.
- **Roof:** Steep clay tile or slate roof (thatch banned in many towns due to fire risk). Gabled end faces the street. A small dormer window may light the attic.
- **Windows:** Upper floors have larger windows than peasant homes -- wooden mullioned frames with small diamond-paned leaded glass (in wealthier houses) or oiled linen. Iron casement hinges. Wooden shutters painted in the household's or guild's colors.
- **Signage:** A painted wooden sign or carved symbol hanging from an iron bracket indicates the trade (a boot for a cobbler, scissors for a tailor, a key for a locksmith).
- **Size:** 15-20 feet wide, 40-60 feet deep, 2-3 stories plus attic.

### Interior Rooms

**Ground Floor -- Shop/Workshop:**
- The front half opens to the street via the counter-shutter
- A long oak counter or display table for showing wares
- Wall-mounted shelving or cubby holes for stored goods
- A lockable strongbox or iron-bound chest for money and valuables, bolted to the floor
- A pair of brass scales and lead weights
- An abacus or counting board
- The back half contains workspace: tool storage, raw materials, work-in-progress
- A narrow staircase (steep, enclosed in wood paneling) leading up
- A passage to the rear yard (which contains a well, privy, and sometimes a small garden)

**First Floor -- Hall/Living Room:**
- The main living and dining space, heated by a stone fireplace built into the party wall (shared chimney stack with neighboring house)
- **Fireplace:** Carved stone surround with iron fire-dogs (andirons), a fire screen, and a mantelpiece displaying pewter candlesticks, a clock (in later periods), or devotional items
- A long oak trestle table (seats 6-8) with turned legs
- Joint stools and a bench along the wall
- A carved oak court cupboard (open-fronted) displaying pewter plates, ceramic jugs, a silver salt cellar (status symbol)
- A settle (high-backed wooden bench) near the fire
- Woven rush matting or a simple woven carpet on the plank floor
- One or two tapestries or painted linen hangings on the walls depicting religious scenes or hunting
- An iron chandelier (ring-type) with 4-6 candle holders suspended from the ceiling beam
- A small devotional niche or prie-dieu (prayer desk) with a painted wooden icon or small brass crucifix

**Second Floor -- Bedchambers:**
- **Master chamber:** A large oak four-poster bed with rope-strung base and a straw-over-feather mattress, linen sheets, a woolen blanket, and a patchwork or embroidered coverlet. Bed curtains (heavy wool or linen) for warmth and privacy. A turned wooden bedpost, sometimes carved. A wooden chest at the foot of the bed for clothing and linens. A wash stand: an oak frame holding a ceramic basin and ewer (pitcher). A small polished steel mirror on the wall. A chamber pot (ceramic or pewter) under the bed. A prayer kneeler.
- **Children's/servant room:** Simpler truckle beds (low beds on wheels that slide under the master bed during the day), shared palliasses, a wooden chest, wall pegs for clothing.

**Attic:**
- Low-ceilinged storage space under the eaves
- Sacks of grain, barrels, dried goods
- A spinning wheel or loom if the household produces cloth
- Old furniture, spare tools

**Cellar/Undercroft (below ground floor):**
- Barrel-vaulted stone ceiling
- Cool and dark -- stores wine barrels, beer casks, wheels of cheese, salted meat
- Stone shelving along walls
- Sometimes a drain channel to the street sewer

### Textures and Colors
- **Stone ground floor:** Grey limestone, soot-stained near the street; mortar visible between courses
- **Timber framing:** Dark brown-to-black painted oak; some carved with rosettes or guild marks
- **Plaster infill:** Off-white to cream, sometimes decorated with painted geometric patterns or false timbering
- **Interior plaster:** Lime-washed white walls with possible painted frieze borders (red ochre, blue-grey)
- **Floor:** Wide oak planks, darkened with age and beeswax polish; gaps between boards visible
- **Furniture:** Dark oak with visible tool marks, some pieces carved with linen-fold paneling or Gothic tracery

### Lighting
- **Ground floor shop:** Good natural light through the open counter-shutter during business hours; dim when shuttered
- **Hall:** Fireplace glow (warm amber), supplemented by candles in the chandelier and pewter candlesticks; daylight from leaded glass windows casts diamond-pattern shadows
- **Bedchambers:** A single candle or rush light; very dim
- **Cellar:** Near total darkness; a single candle lantern carried in

### Atmosphere/Smell Cues
- Street-level: the smell of the trade (leather, metal, baking, dye) mixed with street odors (horse manure, rotting refuse in the central gutter, wood smoke from neighboring chimneys)
- Hall: wood smoke, beeswax polish, tallow candles, cooking aromas drifting from the kitchen below or behind
- Bedchamber: musty linen, dried lavender placed in chests as moth repellent, wood
- Cellar: damp stone, sour wine, mold, cool stillness

## 1.3 Manor House (Wealthy Merchant/Minor Noble)

### Purpose
Residence of a wealthy merchant, guild master, or minor nobleman and their household of 15-30 people (family, servants, retainers). Functions as both a home and an administrative center for local affairs or business operations.

### Exterior Appearance
- **Walls:** Dressed stone (limestone or sandstone) on the ground floor, timber-framed upper stories with plaster infill. Some manors are entirely stone-built. Walls are 2-3 feet thick on the ground floor.
- **Roof:** Steep slate or stone tile roof with multiple gables. Decorative stone finials at the gable peaks. Lead-lined valleys and gutters. Tall chimney stacks of coursed stone or decorative brick.
- **Windows:** Stone-mullioned windows with tracery heads (pointed Gothic arches). Leaded glass in diamond or rectangular panes. The great hall has tall windows along one side. An oriel window (projecting bay window with corbelled stone support) in the solar or great chamber.
- **Entrance:** An arched stone doorway with carved mouldings (dog-tooth or chevron patterns). Heavy oak double doors with iron ring handles and decorative iron strapping. A carved stone coat of arms or merchant's mark above the door.
- **Grounds:** Enclosed courtyard with a stone well, mounting block, and hitching posts. A walled garden to the rear with herb beds, a fish pond, and fruit trees (apple, pear). Outbuildings: kitchen (separate from main house for fire safety), stable, dovecote, brewhouse.
- **Size:** 60-100 feet long, 30-40 feet wide, 2 stories plus attic. The courtyard may be 40x40 feet.

### Interior Rooms

**Screens Passage (Entry):**
- A cross-passage at the lower end of the great hall, separated by a carved oak screen (the "screens passage")
- The screen has two or three arched doorways leading into the hall
- Carved tracery panels on the screen, sometimes painted and gilded
- Coat hooks, a wooden umbrella/sword stand, a stone holy-water stoup by the door
- Flagstone floor

**Great Hall:**
- The social and ceremonial heart of the manor, open to the roof or with a hammer-beam timber ceiling
- **Dimensions:** 40-60 feet long, 20-30 feet wide, 25-35 feet to the roof peak
- **Hearth:** A large central open hearth (earlier period) or a massive stone fireplace at one end with a carved stone or wood overmantel bearing the family's arms. Fire-dogs, a fire-back of cast iron embossed with heraldic devices, and a set of fire tools (poker, tongs, shovel) on a stand
- **High table/Dais:** A raised stone or wooden platform at the upper end, 6 inches high. A long oak table with turned legs, covered with a white linen cloth for meals. The lord's carved oak chair (the only chair with arms and a back) at the center; everyone else has joint stools or benches
- **Lower tables:** Trestle tables and benches for the household, set up for meals and removed after
- **Walls:** Lime-plastered and painted with decorative schemes (heraldic shields, hunt scenes, geometric borders) or hung with tapestries (Flemish-style millefleurs, hunting scenes, biblical narratives). The tapestries also provide insulation.
- **Floor:** Stone flags or decorative tile (encaustic tiles in red, yellow, green, and black with inlaid patterns). Rushes strewn over the floor.
- **Chandelier:** A large wrought-iron or bronze wheel-chandelier suspended from the central beam, holding 12-24 candles
- **Music gallery:** A timber balcony above the screens passage for musicians during feasts
- **Furniture:** A large standing aumbry (cupboard) for plate and drinking vessels; a livery cupboard for bread and drink left out overnight; a pewter or silver salt cellar (the "great salt") marking the division between high and low tables

**Solar/Great Chamber (above the dais end):**
- The lord's private living room and bedroom, accessed by a stone spiral stair or wooden stairs from the dais end
- A large canopied bed with carved posts, embroidered hangings, feather mattress over a straw base, linen sheets, fur coverlets (beaver, fox, or squirrel)
- A prie-dieu (prayer desk) with a painted triptych or illuminated Book of Hours
- A writing desk with inkwell, quills, pounce pot (for drying ink), and a beeswax seal
- A carved chest-on-stand (aumbry) for clothing
- A window seat built into the deep stone window embrasure, with cushions
- A small fireplace (smaller than the hall's)
- A silver hand-mirror, ivory comb, and washbasin on a stand
- A floor rug (imported Eastern carpet or woven English rush carpet)
- Painted cloth hangings or tapestries on the walls

**Buttery:**
- Adjacent to the screens passage at the lower end of the hall
- Rows of wooden shelving holding pewter and silver drinking vessels, jugs, flagons
- Wine barrels (tuns) and beer casks on wooden stillages
- A serving hatch to the hall
- A lockable door (the butler controls access)
- A stone or brick floor, cool temperature

**Pantry:**
- Adjacent to the buttery, also off the screens passage
- Shelves for bread, cheese, butter, dried fruits, spices in locked boxes
- A bread-bin, a cheese press
- Scales and weights
- A wooden chopping block
- The pantler's domain -- locked and inventoried

**Kitchen (separate building or connected wing):**
- A massive stone fireplace (8-12 feet wide) with multiple cooking positions: spit-jacks for roasting, pot hooks, a bread oven built into the wall beside the main fireplace
- A central wooden chopping block table
- Iron pots, copper pans, brass kettles hanging from wall hooks
- A mortar and pestle (stone, large size) for grinding spices
- A spit-turning mechanism (hand-crank or, in later periods, a turnspit dog wheel)
- A stone sink with a drain to the outside
- Shelving with ceramic storage jars, wooden bowls, ladles, flesh-hooks
- Bundles of dried herbs hanging from the ceiling
- A stone floor sloping to a central drain

**Chapel (if present):**
- A small room or attached wing with a stone altar
- A painted wooden reredos (screen behind the altar) depicting saints
- A brass or silver candlestick pair on the altar
- A wooden pew or prayer stalls for the family
- A stained glass window (small, with heraldic or devotional design)
- A piscina (stone basin) for washing communion vessels
- A holy water stoup by the door

**Servants' Quarters:**
- Simpler rooms in the wings or above the service areas
- Shared palliasse beds, a communal chest, wall pegs
- A wash basin and bucket

### Textures and Colors
- **Exterior stone:** Warm honey-gold (Cotswold limestone) or pale grey (granite), weathered with orange lichen on the north face
- **Interior plaster:** White lime wash with painted borders in red ochre, ultramarine blue, verdigris green
- **Timber:** Dark oak, elaborately carved with linen-fold or tracery panels, polished with beeswax to a warm brown sheen
- **Tiles:** Encaustic tiles in deep red, golden yellow, dark green, and black with inlaid heraldic designs
- **Metals:** Polished pewter (soft grey sheen), tarnished silver (warm grey), wrought iron (matte black), brass (warm gold)
- **Textiles:** Rich wools in deep reds, blues, and greens; linen in white and natural flax; imported silk in bold colors for cushions and bed hangings

### Lighting
- **Great Hall:** The wheel-chandelier provides pools of warm candlelight from above; the fireplace casts dancing orange light and deep shadows up the walls; tall windows admit strong daylight on sunny days, with colored light through stained glass segments
- **Solar:** More intimate -- a few candles in brass holders, firelight, and the oriel window providing soft angled light
- **Service rooms:** Dim -- a single candle lantern or rushlight; the kitchen is lit primarily by the massive cooking fire
- **Chapel:** Deliberately atmospheric -- 2-4 candles on the altar, with colored light from the stained glass window creating pools of ruby, sapphire, and emerald on the stone floor

### Atmosphere/Smell Cues
- Great Hall: wood smoke from the hearth, tallow and beeswax from candles, the savory aroma of roasting meat from the kitchen, ale and wine, the earthy scent of fresh rushes mixed with herbs (rosemary, meadowsweet)
- Solar: beeswax polish, lavender from linen chests, parchment and ink, a private fire's wood smoke
- Kitchen: roasting meat, baking bread, wood fire, rendered fat, spices (pepper, ginger, cinnamon), onions
- Buttery: sour wine, yeast from beer casks, damp stone
- Chapel: incense (frankincense), beeswax candles, cold stone, a faint mustiness

## 1.4 Noble Estate (Large, Multiple Wings)

### Purpose
Seat of a powerful lord, baron, or high-ranking noble. Houses 50-200 people including the noble family, household knights, clergy, administrators, and numerous servants. Functions as a political, judicial, and social center for the surrounding region.

### Exterior Appearance
- **Construction:** Entirely stone-built (ashlar-faced limestone or sandstone). Some wings may have timber-framed upper stories. Crenellated parapets on the roofline (a license to crenellate was a mark of royal favor). Corner turrets with conical stone caps.
- **Layout:** Multiple wings arranged around one or two courtyards. The main range contains the great hall and great chamber; the east wing holds the private apartments and chapel; the west wing houses service areas (kitchen, bakehouse, brewhouse); the north range may contain the gatehouse and guest lodgings.
- **Entrance:** A fortified gatehouse with a vaulted passage, heavy oak gates, and possibly a portcullis. A coat of arms carved in stone above the arch. The courtyard within is cobblestoned with a central well or fountain.
- **Grounds:** Formal gardens with hedged partitions, a deer park, fish ponds, orchting orchards, dovecote, falconry mews, stables, a tilt-yard for jousting practice.
- **Scale:** The great hall alone may be 80-100 feet long. The entire complex can cover several acres.

### Interior Rooms

**Gatehouse:**
- Guard chamber on the upper floor with weapon racks, a table for dice, a brazier
- Portcullis winding mechanism (large wooden windlass drum with iron chains)
- Murder holes in the vaulted ceiling above the passage
- Iron-studded oak gates

**Great Hall (enlarged):**
- As per manor house, but larger and more ornate
- Hammer-beam roof with carved angels at each hammer-beam end, painted and gilded
- Minstrels' gallery above the screens passage, with carved wooden balustrade
- Multiple fireplaces (two or more along the long walls)
- Elaborate floor tiles imported from Flanders
- Heraldic banners and pennants hanging from the roof beams
- A salt cellar of silver-gilt (the "nef" -- a ship-shaped salt vessel)

**Great Chamber / Presence Chamber:**
- The lord's audience room for formal meetings and receiving petitions
- A canopy of estate (an elaborate cloth-of-gold or embroidered canopy) over the lord's chair
- A throne-like chair on a dais
- Tapestries covering every wall (Arras tapestries depicting mythological or historical scenes)
- A Persian or Turkish carpet on the floor (extremely rare and valuable)
- A carved stone fireplace with the family arms
- A standing desk for a secretary/clerk

**Private Solar / Withdrawing Room:**
- Smaller, more intimate than the great chamber
- Cushioned window seats in deep embrasures
- A chess set and gaming board
- A portable writing desk (escritoire)
- Musical instruments: a lute, a psaltery, a recorder
- A book chest with a few precious bound manuscripts
- A lapdog's cushion by the fire

**Chapel (dedicated wing):**
- Larger than a manor chapel -- seats 20-30 in carved wooden choir stalls
- A stone altar with a jeweled or enameled altar cross
- A painted rood screen separating the nave from the chancel
- Stained glass windows depicting the family's patron saints
- A brass eagle lectern
- A font for baptisms
- A sacristy (vestry) room off to one side with vestment chests, chalices, patens

**Bedchambers (multiple):**
- **Lord's chamber:** A massive carved bed with a tester (canopy) of embroidered silk, fur-lined hangings, multiple mattresses (straw, wool, and feather layers). A garderobe (built-in wardrobe closet) with a privy shaft. A personal servant's truckle bed nearby.
- **Lady's chamber:** Similarly appointed with an embroidery frame, a spinning wheel, a mirror of polished silver, cosmetics boxes, a jewel casket
- **Guest chambers:** Well-appointed with canopied beds, a wash stand, a candle stand, fresh rushes
- **Children's nursery:** Smaller beds, a cradle, toys (wooden horses, dolls, a spinning top), a nurse's bed

**Long Gallery (later medieval):**
- A narrow room running the length of one wing, used for exercise in bad weather
- Portraits and family heraldic displays on the walls
- A polished wood floor for walking/dancing

**Service Range:**
- Greatly expanded: separate rooms for the baker, brewer, cook, and their assistants
- A massive kitchen with multiple hearths and spit positions
- A wet larder (cool room for fresh meat), dry larder (for preserved goods)
- A spicery (locked room for expensive spices)
- A ewery (for table linen and hand-washing equipment)
- A chandlery (for candle-making and storage)
- A laundry with copper boiling vats

### Textures and Colors
- **Stone:** Pale golden ashlar, precision-cut with tight joints; carved details on window surrounds and door arches
- **Interior walls:** Vibrant painted plaster -- deep red (vermillion), royal blue (ultramarine from lapis lazuli), gold leaf on carved bosses and capitals
- **Timber:** Richly carved oak in hammer-beam roofs, with painted and gilded details; linenfold paneling in the private rooms
- **Floor:** Encaustic tiles in the hall; wide oak boards in the chambers, some with checkered patterns; stone flags in service areas
- **Metalwork:** Gilded bronze chandeliers, silver-gilt plate, polished steel mirrors, elaborate wrought-iron hinges and door furniture

### Lighting
- **Great Hall:** Spectacular -- multiple chandeliers with dozens of beeswax candles, huge fireplace, tall windows flooding the space with daylight. At night, the candlelight creates a golden glow above while the lower corners fall into deep shadow.
- **Private rooms:** More controlled -- brass candlesticks with beeswax candles, a personal fire, window embrasures casting angled light across tapestries
- **Chapel:** Deliberately reverent -- candles on the altar and in wall sconces create warm pools; stained glass paints the stone floor with color during the day
- **Service areas:** Functional -- the kitchen lit by its massive fires; storage rooms by carried lanterns

### Atmosphere/Smell Cues
- Great Hall: roasting meat, spiced wine (hippocras), wood smoke, beeswax, the musk of gathered humanity, fresh rushes with strewing herbs
- Private chambers: rose water, lavender, ambergris-scented pomanders hung to combat disease, parchment, imported incense
- Chapel: frankincense, myrrh, beeswax, cold stone, the faint metallic scent of old brass
- Kitchen: intense heat, roasting fat, bread baking, wood smoke, onions, garlic, the sharp tang of vinegar
- Stables (adjacent): horse sweat, hay, leather, manure, oiled metal (tack)

## 2.1 Tavern/Inn

### Purpose
Combination public drinking house, eatery, and lodging for travelers. The ground floor serves as the common room for local regulars and passing traders. Upper floors provide sleeping rooms for paying guests. The innkeeper and family typically live in rooms behind or above the common area. Also serves as an informal news exchange, hiring hall, and deal-making venue.

### Exterior Appearance
- **Structure:** Timber-framed with jettied upper stories, or stone ground floor with timber above. Wider than a standard townhouse (20-30 feet frontage) to accommodate the common room.
- **Signboard:** A large painted wooden sign on a wrought-iron bracket projecting over the street -- depicting the inn's name (The Prancing Hart, The Broken Crown, The Black Boar). The sign creaks on its hinges in the wind.
- **Door:** Wide double doors of thick oak planks, iron-studded, standing open during business hours. A lantern hook beside the door for a horn lantern lit at night.
- **Windows:** Leaded glass in small panes on the upper floors; the ground floor may have larger shuttered openings that fold open to let in air and allow patrons to lean on the sill
- **Stable yard:** A gated arch to one side leads to a rear courtyard with a stable block, a well, and a manure pile. Hay loft above the stables.
- **Brewery attachment:** Some inns brew their own ale in an attached brewhouse with a visible chimney or copper vent pipe.

### Interior Rooms

**Common Room (Ground Floor):**
- **Floor:** Wide oak planks, heavily scarred with boot marks, knife gouges, and dark stains from spilled ale. Sawdust or rushes strewn to absorb spills.
- **Hearth:** A large stone fireplace at one end with a crackling fire. An iron cooking crane swings a blackened cauldron of perpetual stew over the flames. Fire-dogs and a poker. The mantelpiece is scorched and bears tally marks carved by regulars.
- **Tables:** 4-8 heavy oak trestle tables with bench seating, the wood worn smooth and dark from years of use. One round "corner table" in a darker nook for private conversations. A long communal table for larger groups.
- **Bar/Serving area:** A thick oak counter (the "bar") at one end, behind which stand:
  - Rows of wooden and ceramic ale mugs on shelves
  - Several large wooden casks (hogsheads) on a raised wooden stillage, each with a wooden tap/spigot
  - Pewter tankards, leather jacks (drinking vessels), and clay cups
  - A wooden drawer for coins
  - A slate board or wooden tally sticks for keeping tabs
- **Walls:** Dark-stained plaster between exposed timber beams. Hung with:
  - A boar's head or stag antlers mounted as a trophy
  - Iron hooks with hanging dried sausages, a ham haunch
  - A battered lute or fiddle hung on a peg (for entertainment)
  - A crude painted notice board or wanted posters
  - A dart board (a cross-section of log with scored rings)
- **Ceiling:** Low, with heavy blackened beams from decades of smoke. Iron hooks for hanging lanterns.
- **Lighting:** Tallow candles in iron sconces on the walls, a wrought-iron chandelier with 6-8 candles over the main area, and the fireplace glow. Pools of warm light separated by pockets of shadow.
- **Other:** A wooden staircase at the back (steep, narrow, creaking) leads upstairs. A door beside the bar leads to the kitchen.

**Kitchen (behind the common room):**
- A brick or stone bread oven built into the wall
- A large preparation table scarred with knife cuts
- Shelving with ceramic bowls, wooden platters, iron pots
- A wooden chopping block with cleavers
- Hanging herbs, garlic braids, onion strings from ceiling hooks
- A barrel of salted fish, a crock of lard, a flour bin
- A stone sink or wooden washing trough
- The permanent stew pot on a trivet

**Sleeping Rooms (Upper Floor):**
- **Common sleeping room:** The cheapest option -- a large open room with 6-10 straw palliasses on the floor side by side, a shared wool blanket per bed. Wall pegs for cloaks. A single candle stub in an iron holder. Snoring strangers, fleas, and little privacy.
- **Private rooms (2-4):** Small rooms partitioned by thin wooden walls or curtains. Each contains: a narrow rope-strung bed with a straw mattress, a wool blanket and a linen sheet, a three-legged stool, a wall peg for clothing, a chamber pot under the bed, a candle on a small shelf, and a wooden latch (no lock, or a simple bolt).
- **The best room:** Slightly larger with a small fireplace, a proper bedframe with curtains, a washstand with basin and ewer, and a locking door.

**Cellar:**
- Stone-walled and barrel-vaulted
- Wine casks, beer barrels, cider barrels
- Racks for wine bottles (in later periods)
- A cool temperature year-round
- A trapdoor in the common room floor with a heavy iron ring pull

**Stable Yard:**
- 4-8 wooden stalls for horses
- Hay racks and water troughs
- A tack room with saddles on wooden horses, bridles on hooks
- A manure pile in the corner
- A water pump or well

### Textures and Colors
- **Wood:** Everything is dark -- centuries of smoke, spilled ale, and body oil have darkened every surface to a deep brown-black. The bar top is almost black, polished smooth by countless elbows.
- **Walls:** Dirty cream plaster between dark beams, stained yellow-brown near the ceiling from smoke
- **Floor:** Dark oak with visible grain, scored and pitted; lighter patches where tables and benches usually sit
- **Metal:** Blackened iron (pot hooks, chandelier, hinges); dull pewter mugs with a soft grey sheen
- **Ceramic:** Earthenware mugs and bowls in natural terracotta with green or brown glaze
- **Upstairs:** Rough-sawn timber walls, unplastered; straw bedding in natural gold-brown

### Lighting
- **Common room (evening):** Warm, golden, flickering -- the fire and candles create an atmosphere where faces glow in the foreground and the corners are lost in shadow. The smoke layer hangs at head height, visible in the candlelight.
- **Common room (day):** Brighter near the windows, but the interior is still dim and warm-toned
- **Sleeping rooms:** A single candle or none at all -- near darkness
- **Cellar:** Pitch black; only a carried candle lantern
- **Stable yard:** Open sky; at night, a single lantern by the stable door

### Atmosphere/Smell Cues
- Common room: wood smoke, spilled ale (sweet, yeasty), roasting meat, tallow candles (slightly acrid), sweat and damp wool from travelers, the earthy sweetness of straw and sawdust on the floor
- Kitchen: cooking fat, onions, baking bread, wood smoke, a slightly rancid undertone from old grease
- Sleeping rooms: stale sweat, straw, damp wool, the faint sweet smell of a tallow candle stub
- Cellar: cool damp stone, the fruity/sour smell of fermenting ale, oak barrel wood
- Stable: hay, horse sweat, leather, manure -- warm and pungent

## 2.2 Blacksmith/Forge

### Purpose
Workshop for a blacksmith who produces and repairs iron and steel goods: horseshoes, nails, hinges, tools, weapons, and armor components. The forge is the economic anchor of any settlement, essential for agricultural and military life. The smith is among the most respected artisans.

### Exterior Appearance
- **Structure:** Stone or brick walls (fire-resistant necessity), single story with a high-pitched roof. Often partially open-sided or with a wide front opening sheltered by a timber lean-to roof, allowing heat to escape and permitting the smith to work visible to the street.
- **Chimney:** A large stone or brick chimney hood over the forge, with a clay or brick flue -- the most prominent exterior feature. Smoke and sparks constantly rise from it.
- **Roof:** Clay tile or slate (never thatch -- fire risk). The roof has ventilation gaps or a louvered ridge vent to release heat.
- **Door:** A wide opening (6-8 feet) with no door, or heavy wooden double doors that stand open during work. Iron horseshoes nailed to the doorframe for luck.
- **Exterior details:** Finished goods displayed on hooks outside -- horseshoes, chains, fire pokers. A large flat stone or tree stump for cooling/testing pieces. A water trough for horses near the entrance.
- **Size:** 20-30 feet square, with a high ceiling (12-16 feet) for ventilation.
- **Yard:** A pile of coal or charcoal beside the building. A stack of iron bar stock and scrap metal. A quenching trough outside.

### Interior -- Single Large Workshop

**The Forge (Fire):**
- A raised stone or brick hearth (3-4 feet high, 3-4 feet square) positioned slightly off-center, with a deep fire pot lined with firebrick
- A stone or brick chimney hood above, funneling smoke through a flue to the outside
- The fire burns coal or charcoal, glowing orange-white at the center
- A tuyere (air inlet pipe) enters the fire from below or the side, connected to:

**The Bellows:**
- A large double-chambered leather bellows (4-6 feet long) mounted on a wooden frame beside the forge
- Operated by a long wooden handle pumped by hand or foot
- Connected to the forge by an iron pipe (tuyere)
- The bellows wheeze and gasp with each pump, the leather creaking

**The Anvil:**
- A massive wrought-iron anvil (100-300 pounds) mounted on a thick oak or elm stump driven into the earth floor
- Positioned 2-3 feet from the forge for quick transfer of hot metal
- The face is polished smooth from hammering; the horn (pointed end) is used for bending curves
- A hardy hole (square) and pritchel hole (round) in the face for inserting tools

**Tool Storage:**
- **Wall-mounted rack:** Tongs of various sizes (flat-jawed, round-jawed, scroll tongs, bolt tongs -- 8-12 pairs minimum), hanging from iron hooks
- **Hammer rack:** Cross-peen hammers, ball-peen hammers, sledgehammers, set hammers, planishing hammers (6-10 total)
- **Chisel/punch rack:** Hot chisels, cold chisels, center punches, drifts, fullers, swages, all organized by size
- **Hardies:** Bottom tools that fit into the anvil's hardy hole -- cutters, fullers, swages -- stored in a wooden rack nearby

**The Slack Tub:**
- A large wooden barrel or stone trough filled with water (sometimes brine or oil) for quenching hot metal
- Positioned on the opposite side of the anvil from the forge
- The water is perpetually warm and slightly oily, with a thin film of iron scale floating on the surface

**Workbench:**
- A heavy oak or elm workbench along one wall for cold-work: filing, grinding, fitting
- A leg vise (iron jaws on an oak post) bolted to the bench for holding work
- Files (flat, half-round, rat-tail) in a wooden rack
- A grinding stone (foot-operated treadle grindstone) nearby
- Wire brushes, scrapers, punches arranged on the bench

**Floor:**
- Packed earth or flagstone -- scorched and pitted with burn marks
- Iron filings, slag chips, and coal dust scattered everywhere
- A drain channel to carry water from the quench tub

**Storage Area (rear or side):**
- Raw iron bar stock leaning against the wall in bundles
- Scrap iron in a pile for recycling
- Coal or charcoal in a wooden bin or stone-walled bay
- Finished goods on shelves: horseshoes, nails in wooden kegs, hinges, chains, hooks

### Textures and Colors
- **Dominant palette:** Black (soot, coal, iron), orange-red (hot metal, fire), dark grey (cold iron, stone)
- **Stone/brick:** Fire-blackened on interior surfaces near the forge; the chimney hood is caked with soot
- **Iron:** From glowing white-yellow (welding heat) through cherry red, dark red, to black (cold) -- every stage visible simultaneously
- **Wood:** The anvil stump and bellows frame are darkened and scorched; the workbench is scarred with burns and hammer dents
- **Water:** The slack tub water is grey-brown, with iridescent oil sheen and floating black scale

### Lighting
- **Primary:** The forge fire itself -- an intense, pulsing orange-red glow that illuminates the center of the space and casts dramatic shadows. When the bellows are pumped, the fire flares white-hot and the whole room brightens.
- **Secondary:** Daylight through the open front and high windows/vents. On an overcast day, the forge is the primary light source.
- **Contrast:** Extreme -- the brightest point (the fire) is inches from the darkest shadows. Hot iron glows in the dark corners. The smith's face is underlit by the forge, creating dramatic hollows.

### Atmosphere/Smell Cues
- **Dominant:** The sharp, metallic tang of hot iron -- a searing, acrid smell that catches in the throat
- Coal or charcoal smoke -- slightly sweet (charcoal) or acrid (coal)
- The hiss and rush of steam when hot metal hits the quench water
- The rhythmic clang-clang-clang of hammer on anvil, echoing off the stone walls
- Scorched earth and hot stone from the forge base
- Horse hoof (burnt keratin -- a strong, unpleasant smell when shoeing horses)
- Sweat and leather from the smith's heavy apron
- Oil from the slake tub
- Heat: the air near the forge shimmers; the rest of the shop feels cool by comparison

## 2.3 Apothecary/Alchemist Shop

### Purpose
Part pharmacy, part herbalist, part proto-chemist. The apothecary prepares and sells medicines, salves, tinctures, and remedies from herbs, minerals, and animal products. In a dark fantasy setting, the line between medicine and magic blurs -- the shop also sells questionable potions, protective charms, and ingredients for rituals. The apothecary may also be consulted for diagnosis and treatment.

### Exterior Appearance
- **Structure:** A narrow timber-framed townhouse, 2 stories. The ground floor shop front has a hinged counter-shutter that folds down to display wares. A small projecting sign shows the symbol of the profession: a mortar and pestle, a serpent coiling around a staff, or an alembic.
- **Distinctive feature:** Bundles of dried herbs and roots hang from the eaves or from hooks around the shop front, giving the exterior a wild, organic appearance. Pots of living herbs (rosemary, sage, lavender, hyssop) sit on the window ledge.
- **Windows:** Small leaded glass panes, some tinted green or amber from impurities in the glass. Dried specimens or colored glass bottles visible in the window.
- **Door:** A narrow door with a small bell on a spring that rings when opened. The doorframe is carved with apotropaic symbols (protective marks against evil).
- **Smell:** The exterior smells of dried herbs, camphor, and something vaguely chemical even from the street.

### Interior Rooms

**Shop Front (Ground Floor Front):**
- **Floor:** Worn oak planks, darkened with age, with scattered dried herb leaves
- **Counter:** A long oak counter with a polished top, behind which the apothecary works. On the counter:
  - A large brass mortar and pestle (the primary tool -- used constantly)
  - A small brass balance scale with a set of nested brass weights
  - A marble slab for mixing ointments
  - A wooden pill-rolling board
  - A recipe book (handwritten, leather-bound, stained and dog-eared)
- **Wall shelves (floor to ceiling):** The defining visual of the space. Dozens of ceramic apothecary jars (albarelli) in blue-and-white majolica or brown salt-glazed stoneware, each labeled in Latin with its contents. Interspersed with:
  - Green and amber glass bottles of tinctures and distilled spirits
  - Wooden boxes with sliding lids for powders
  - Bundles of dried herbs tied with twine
  - Dried animal specimens: a crocodile or lizard hung from the ceiling as a sign of the exotic, dried starfish, seahorses in jars
  - Small drawers in a wooden cabinet (an "apothecary chest") with 30-50 tiny labeled drawers for individual ingredients

**Laboratory/Preparation Room (Ground Floor Rear):**
- **Distillation apparatus:** A brick or stone furnace (athanor) with a glass or copper alembic (distillation head) and a receiving flask -- connected by glass or copper tubing. Multiple retorts and flasks in various stages of distillation.
- **Drying rack:** A wooden frame with stretched linen for drying herbs, suspended from the ceiling
- **Work table:** A heavy oak table with:
  - A cutting board and herb knife
  - A stone mortar (larger than the shop one) for heavy grinding
  - Ceramic crucibles for heating mineral ingredients
  - Wax seals and string for sealing bottles
  - Small glass phials ready for filling
- **Water source:** A stone cistern or large ceramic jar of distilled water
- **Storage:** Shelves with bulk ingredients -- bags of dried chamomile, lavender, valerian root, mandrake root (in a locked box), quicksilver in a sealed container, sulfur in a jar, alum, saltpeter
- **Curiosities (dark fantasy):** A jar of glowing fungus, a preserved imp or fairy in a jar of spirits, a moonstone in a velvet pouch, bone dust in a mortar

**Upper Floor -- Living Quarters:**
- A small bedchamber and sitting room for the apothecary
- Bookshelves crammed with herbals, bestiaries, and alchemical treatises
- More shelving with overflow stock and rare ingredients
- A writing desk with quill, ink, and patient records

### Textures and Colors
- **Dominant palette:** Dark wood, amber glass, blue-white ceramic, dried botanical greens and browns
- **Shelving:** Dark oak, crowded and slightly bowed under weight
- **Jars:** Glossy ceramic in cobalt blue with white lettering; amber and green glass catching the light
- **Herbs:** Every shade of dried green, brown, and gold; some deep purple (lavender), bright yellow (turmeric), dark red (saffron)
- **Counter:** Stained with decades of spills -- rings from bottles, dark spots from tinctures, a slight wax residue

### Lighting
- **Shop front:** Daylight filtered through small windows and the open counter-shutter; the interior is deliberately dim to protect light-sensitive ingredients. The colored glass bottles create warm amber and green glows where light passes through.
- **Laboratory:** Dimmer still -- lit by the glow from the athanor furnace (warm orange) and a single candle. The distillation apparatus catches and refracts the light.
- **Overall mood:** Intimate, cluttered, slightly mysterious -- light and shadow play among the hundreds of objects

### Atmosphere/Smell Cues
- The most aromatic building in the settlement
- Layers of scent: dried lavender and rosemary (top notes), camphor and menthol (sharp medicinal middle), sulfur and mineral salts (undertone), old leather and parchment (the books)
- A faint chemical tang from distillation processes
- Beeswax from sealed jars and candles
- In a dark fantasy context: an occasional whiff of something wrong -- ozone, rot, or a metallic blood-like scent from forbidden ingredients
- The air feels thick, slightly warm, and heavy with botanical dust

## 2.4 General Goods Store / Market Stall

### Purpose
Sells a variety of everyday goods that households need: rope, candles, soap, cloth, needles, pins, thread, pots, pans, tools, salt, dried foods, lanterns, nails, leather goods. In a permanent shop, the merchant is a middleman buying from craftsmen and importing from other towns. Market stalls are temporary, erected on market days (1-2 days per week).

### Exterior Appearance -- Permanent Shop
- **Structure:** Ground floor of a timber-framed townhouse, similar to other merchant buildings
- **Shop front:** A large hinged counter-shutter folds down to create a display counter. Goods hang from hooks above and around the opening: coils of rope, bundles of candles, iron pots, leather bags
- **Signage:** A painted sign showing a key, a pair of scales, or a bundle of goods

### Exterior Appearance -- Market Stall
- **Frame:** A simple timber frame (four posts, two crossbeams, a sloped canvas or oiled-linen roof)
- **Counter:** A wide plank laid across two trestles at waist height
- **Display:** Baskets and crates arranged on and around the counter. Larger items (barrels, sacks) behind. A canvas backdrop tied to the rear posts.
- **Size:** 6-10 feet wide, 4-6 feet deep

### Interior -- Permanent Shop

**Shop Floor:**
- Crowded and cluttered -- every surface holds merchandise
- **Ceiling:** Iron hooks holding coils of rope, bundles of tallow candles, strings of dried herbs, tin lanterns, leather pouches, iron chains
- **Shelving (wall-to-wall):** Bolts of cloth (wool, linen, canvas) stacked vertically; boxes of needles, pins, buttons, buckles; ceramic pots of soap; jars of tallow; bundles of beeswax
- **Counter:** A heavy oak counter with a built-in brass scale and weights. A wooden cash drawer or lockbox. A large leather-bound ledger for accounts. Quill and inkwell.
- **Floor stock:** Barrels of nails, barrel of salt, sacks of dried peas and beans, a stack of wooden buckets, a crate of clay pipes
- **Wall pegs:** Iron tools (hammers, hatchets, files), leather belts, pouches, and bags
- **Display basket:** On the counter -- an assortment of small, impulse-buy items: combs, small knives, flints, tinderboxes, dice, small mirrors, ribbons

**Back Room:**
- Additional stock stored in crates and barrels
- A work table for measuring and cutting cloth
- A set of scales for weighing bulk goods
- A writing desk for correspondence and ordering

### Textures and Colors
- A riot of different textures: rough canvas, smooth linen, coarse rope, cold iron, warm wood, glossy ceramic, dull pewter
- Colors are muted and earthy: undyed cloth in cream and brown, natural rope in pale gold, dark iron, warm wood tones, with occasional bright spots (dyed ribbons, painted pottery)

### Lighting
- Bright near the open shopfront; progressively darker toward the rear
- Goods in the shadows are hard to see without asking the shopkeeper to bring a lantern

### Atmosphere/Smell Cues
- An eclectic mix: tallow (waxy, slightly animal), rope hemp (dusty, grassy), leather (rich, tannic), soap (lye, tallow), dried herbs, metal (cold iron tang), musty cloth, the sharp scent of vinegar from preserved goods
- The sound of clinking metal, rustling cloth, and the shopkeeper's patter

## 2.5 Bakery

### Purpose
Produces bread (the dietary staple), pies, pastries, and sometimes biscuits/hard-tack for travelers and military. The baker rises before dawn to fire the oven and have fresh bread ready by morning. In many towns, the baker also operates a communal oven where households bring dough to be baked for a fee.

### Exterior Appearance
- **Structure:** Stone or brick (fire-resistant), single story with a high-roofed baking area. The massive chimney of the bread oven dominates the roofline. The building radiates warmth even in winter.
- **Shop front:** A wide counter-shutter opens to display the day's bread. Loaves are stacked on wooden shelves visible from the street. A small handwritten sign (or picture of a wheat sheaf) marks the bakery.
- **Oven chimney:** The largest exterior feature -- a wide stone or brick chimney, often slightly cracked and permanently stained with soot. Smoke and the smell of baking drift from it continuously.
- **Size:** 15-25 feet wide, 30-40 feet deep.

### Interior Rooms

**Shop Front:**
- A wooden counter with a scale and weights
- Shelving behind the counter displaying the day's loaves: round boules, long manchet loaves (fine white flour for the wealthy), dark maslin loaves (mixed grain for commoners), trencher bread (flat, stale loaves used as plates)
- Baskets of bread rolls, meat pies, fruit tarts
- A wooden bread rack on the wall
- A moneybox or wooden drawer for coins

**Baking Room (the heart of the bakery):**
- **The Oven:** A massive domed brick or stone oven (6-8 feet wide, 3-4 feet high dome interior) built against or into the rear wall. The oven mouth is a low arched opening at waist height. A heavy iron door seals it during baking. The oven is heated by burning bundles of faggots (brushwood) inside, then raking out the coals and inserting the bread directly onto the hot brick floor.
- **Long-handled wooden peels** (5-8 feet long flat paddles) for inserting and removing loaves -- 3-4 of different sizes leaning against the wall
- **Kneading trough:** A large wooden trough (6 feet long, 2 feet wide, 18 inches deep) where dough is mixed and kneaded. Flour-dusted and deeply grooved from use.
- **Work table:** A heavy oak or elm table, thickly dusted with flour, with a rolling pin, dough scrapers, and shaping tools
- **Rising shelves:** Wooden shelves covered with linen cloths where shaped loaves proof (rise) before baking
- **Flour storage:** Large wooden bins or barrels of flour (white, whole-meal, rye). A fine mesh sieve (bolting cloth stretched on a wooden frame) for sifting flour.
- **Water bucket and measuring jugs**
- **Yeast crock:** A ceramic jar of sourdough starter or brewer's yeast barm
- **Salt box** on the wall
- **Faggot storage:** Bundles of dried brushwood stacked against a wall for oven fuel

**Floor:** Stone flags, perpetually dusted with flour. Near the oven, the stone is scorched and cracked from heat.

### Textures and Colors
- **Dominant:** Warm tones -- golden-brown crusted bread, pale cream flour dust on every surface, warm red-brown brick of the oven, golden firelight
- **Flour:** A fine white-to-cream dust coats every surface, fills the air, and settles in every crack
- **Bread:** Every shade of golden-brown to deep mahogany-dark crusts
- **Brick/stone:** The oven interior is blackened; the exterior is warm red-brown fading to sooty grey at the chimney
- **Wood:** Pale from flour dust, worn smooth from decades of kneading

### Lighting
- **Primary:** The oven mouth when open -- a blast of orange-red heat-light that illuminates the baking room with a warm, shifting glow
- **Secondary:** Daylight from the shop-front opening and high windows. Flour dust hangs in the light beams like gold motes.
- **Pre-dawn baking:** The baker works by the oven's glow and a single tallow candle -- dramatic light and shadow

### Atmosphere/Smell Cues
- **The best smell in the medieval town:** Fresh baking bread -- warm, yeasty, sweet, and deeply comforting
- Flour dust (dry, slightly sweet, tickles the nose)
- Burning wood from the oven (clean, smoky)
- The sour tang of active yeast cultures
- Intense dry heat radiating from the oven -- the baking room is always warm, even in winter
- The baker's sweat -- it is grueling physical work in extreme heat

## 2.6 Tannery (Leather Worker)

### Purpose
Converts raw animal hides into usable leather through a multi-step process involving soaking, liming, scraping, and tanning with bark or other agents. Produces leather for shoemakers, saddlers, bookbinders, and armorsmiths. Universally located outside town walls or in a designated district because of the extraordinary stench.

### Exterior Appearance
- **Location:** Along a river or stream, downwind and downstream from the main settlement. Often clustered with other noxious trades (dyers, fullers, slaughterhouses).
- **Structure:** Low, sprawling timber-framed buildings around a yard of sunken pits. Not a single building but a complex: workspace, pit yard, drying loft, and storage.
- **Pit yard:** The defining feature -- 6-12 stone-lined or wooden-lined rectangular pits (4-6 feet square, 3-4 feet deep) sunk into the ground, each containing different solutions at different stages of the process. The pits are arranged in rows.
- **Drying frames:** Tall wooden A-frame or rectangular stretching frames in the yard where partially processed hides are stretched to dry, looking like pale, irregular flags.
- **Drying loft:** A large open-sided upper floor (like a barn loft) with ventilation slats, where finished leather is hung to cure. Hides drape from wooden poles.
- **Stream/channel:** A water channel diverts stream water into the yard for washing and draining away waste.
- **Visual impression:** Grim and industrial -- wet hides draped everywhere, murky brown pit water, scraped flesh and hair scattered, muddy ground, buzzing flies.

### Interior Rooms

**Workshop (Ground Floor):**
- **Floor:** Packed earth or stone flags, permanently wet and slippery with animal fat, tanning liquor, and waste. A drainage channel runs through the center.
- **Beam/fleshing horse:** A large wooden beam (the "beam") at waist height, curved and smooth, over which hides are draped for scraping. Beside it:
  - A two-handled fleshing knife (curved, blunt-edged) for removing flesh from the hide's underside
  - A two-handled unhairing knife (sharper) for scraping off loosened hair after liming
  - A moon knife (half-moon shaped) for detailed work
- **Lime pit tools:** Long wooden paddles for stirring hides in the lime pits
- **Bark mill:** A simple stone or wooden mill for grinding oak bark (or other tanbark) into powder
- **Storage:** Baskets of ground tanbark, buckets of lime paste, jars of animal brains (used in brain tanning), containers of alum and salt
- **Finishing bench:** A flat wooden table where finished leather is:
  - Rubbed smooth with a slick stone or glass
  - Oiled with fish oil or tallow for waterproofing
  - Cut to size with leather shears

**The Pits (open yard -- the process stages):**
- **Pit 1-2 (Soaking):** Fresh hides soaked in water to rehydrate and remove blood, dirt, and salt (from preservation). Water is murky brown-red.
- **Pit 3-4 (Liming):** Hides submerged in a slurry of water and slaked lime (calcium hydroxide), sometimes with wood ash. This loosens the hair and swells the hide. The solution is thick, white-grey, and caustic. Hides soak for 1-2 weeks.
- **Pit 5-6 (Bating/Puering):** After liming and scraping, hides are treated with a solution of animal dung (dog dung was preferred, also pigeon) to soften them. This is the foulest-smelling stage.
- **Pit 7-12 (Tanning):** Hides are layered with crushed oak bark in cold water and left for 6-18 months. The solution is dark brown. Multiple pits at different stages of maturity.

**Drying Loft:**
- Open-sided structure above the workshop
- Wooden poles running horizontally with finished leather draped over them
- Good airflow through slatted walls
- The leather darkens as it dries from pale pink-white to warm brown

### Textures and Colors
- **Hides:** White-grey (fresh), swollen translucent (limed), warm tan-brown (tanned), deep brown or black (finished)
- **Pit liquor:** Chalky white (lime), murky brown (bark tan), greenish-grey (dung bate)
- **Ground:** Muddy, dark brown, slick with animal fats and tanning liquor
- **Wood:** All wooden surfaces are stained dark brown-black by tannins
- **Bark:** Dark reddish-brown in chips and powder

### Lighting
- **Mostly outdoors:** The pit yard is open to the sky. The workshop has wide openings for ventilation. Natural light prevails.
- **Drying loft:** Strips of light through the ventilation slats create a barred pattern across the hanging hides

### Atmosphere/Smell Cues
- **The worst smell in the medieval settlement -- by far**
- Rotting flesh and blood from raw hides
- Lime (caustic, chemical, nose-burning)
- Animal dung (the bating pits are unspeakable)
- The thick, heavy, slightly sweet smell of tanning bark
- Rancid animal fat
- Flies buzzing constantly over the pits and waste piles
- A coating of greasy moisture on every surface
- Workers develop a permanent smell that clings to their clothing and skin
- Even downwind, the tannery can be detected from hundreds of yards away

## 2.7 Butcher Shop

### Purpose
Slaughters animals and sells fresh and preserved meat. The butcher receives livestock, slaughters in the rear yard or at a separate shambles (slaughterhouse), then butchers the carcasses and sells cuts from the shop front. Also produces sausages, salted meat, and smoked meat.

### Exterior Appearance
- **Structure:** Stone ground floor (for coolness and cleanup), timber upper story for storage/living
- **Shop front:** Wide counter-shutter with iron meat hooks protruding above, from which hang whole carcasses and large cuts. A heavy wooden chopping block is visible at the counter.
- **Blood gutter:** A stone channel in the street outside the shop carries blood and wash water to the central sewer. The cobblestones in front are permanently stained dark.
- **Flies:** Ever-present in warm weather, despite the butcher's best efforts
- **Rear yard:** A walled killing yard with a drain, a hoist beam for hanging carcasses, and a blood pit

### Interior

**Shop/Cutting Room:**
- **Floor:** Flagstone, sloped to a central drain. Sawdust or straw strewn to absorb blood.
- **The Block:** A massive round or rectangular chopping block (a cross-section of elm or sycamore trunk, 18-24 inches thick, 3-4 feet across) on stout legs. Deeply scarred with cleaver marks. The surface is scrubbed with salt.
- **Meat hooks:** Iron hooks suspended from a ceiling beam or wall-mounted rack. Whole sides of beef, pork, or mutton hang from these. Rabbits, pheasants, and other small game hang by their feet.
- **Tools (wall-mounted rack):**
  - A heavy cleaver (for splitting bones and joints)
  - A boning knife (thin, flexible blade)
  - Skinning knives (short, curved)
  - A long slicing knife for cutting steaks and chops
  - A bone saw (bow-shaped, with a fine-toothed blade)
  - A steel (sharpening rod) hung on a leather thong
  - A meat hook (long, with a wooden handle)
- **Display:** A wooden counter with the day's cuts arranged on wooden trays: chops, joints, offal (liver, kidneys, heart, tripe), sausage links, a bowl of dripping (rendered fat)
- **Preserving corner:** A salt box, a barrel of brine, strings of hanging sausages in various stages of drying, a smoking box or connection to a smokehouse

**Back Room/Storage:**
- A cool stone-walled room (the "cold store") for hanging meat
- Barrels of salt for curing
- Strings of dried herbs (sage, thyme) for sausage making
- A sausage-stuffing horn and mincing bowl
- Linen wraps and wicker baskets for carrying purchases

### Textures and Colors
- **Meat:** Deep red (fresh beef), pink (pork), pale pink (poultry), dark red-brown (cured/dried)
- **Blood:** Bright red (fresh) to dark brown-black (dried on the floor and block)
- **Bone:** Pale cream-white, sometimes pink where freshly cut
- **Wood:** The chopping block is bleached white on top from salt scrubbing, dark red-brown on the sides from absorbed blood
- **Metal:** Bright steel (freshly sharpened edges), dark iron (hooks), with a thin residue of grease
- **Stone floor:** Grey flags with dark stains that never fully wash out

### Lighting
- **Shop front:** Bright -- the counter-shutter opens wide and the interior is visible to passing customers
- **Back room:** Dim and cool -- little light, to keep meat at a lower temperature

### Atmosphere/Smell Cues
- Raw meat: metallic, slightly sweet, coppery blood smell
- Dried blood: a thicker, more intense metallic scent
- Rendered fat (dripping): warm, greasy, animal
- Fresh sawdust or straw: cuts the meat smell slightly
- Smoked meat: a pleasant, rich, smoky sweetness from the smokehouse
- Salt: a clean, mineral bite in the air
- In warm weather: an undercurrent of early decay, particularly from offal
- Flies buzzing; the wet slap of meat on the block; the crack of the cleaver through bone

## 2.8 Tailor/Weaver

### Purpose
The tailor cuts and sews garments from cloth; the weaver produces cloth from thread on a loom. These may be separate shops or combined in one household where the weaver produces fabric upstairs and the tailor works on the ground floor. The shop serves everyone from peasants (simple tunics) to nobles (elaborate fitted garments).

### Exterior Appearance
- **Structure:** Standard timber-framed townhouse, 2 stories. Upper floor wider (jettied) for the loom room -- looms need width.
- **Shop front:** Counter-shutter displaying finished garments on wooden stands or draped over pegs. Bolts of cloth in various colors visible inside. A pair of large scissors or shears may serve as the trade sign.
- **Color:** Dyed cloth in the window gives the shop a more colorful appearance than its neighbors -- rolls of madder-red, woad-blue, weld-yellow, and undyed cream-white linen

### Interior Rooms

**Ground Floor -- Tailor's Workshop:**
- **Cutting table:** A long, wide oak table (6-8 feet long, 3 feet wide) at waist height, scarred with pin marks and scissor scratches. Bolts of cloth unrolled across it for cutting.
- **Tools on the table:**
  - Heavy iron shears (12-18 inches long) -- the tailor's primary tool
  - A smaller pair of trimming scissors
  - Pins in a cushion (bronze or bone pins)
  - Chalk or soapstone for marking patterns on cloth
  - A flat wooden ruler and a cord measure
  - A thimble (brass or bone)
  - Needles stored in a small fabric roll
- **Thread and trim:** Spools of linen and silk thread on a wooden rack, sorted by color. Small boxes of buttons (bone, wood, or pewter), buckles, hooks, and ribbon.
- **Dress forms:** 2-3 crude wooden torso forms (padded with straw) for fitting garments in progress
- **Irons:** A heavy flat iron heated on a small brazier for pressing seams
- **Fabric storage:** Bolts of cloth stacked on shelves along the walls, organized by type (wool, linen, silk) and color. The better fabrics are kept in locked cabinets.
- **Fitting area:** A cleared space with a low stool for the client to stand on while being measured and fitted. A small polished metal mirror propped on a stand.

**Upper Floor -- Loom Room (if weaver):**
- **The loom:** A large horizontal floor loom (8-10 feet wide, 6-8 feet deep) dominates the room. The warp threads stretch from a beam at the back to a breast beam at the front. Heddles (harness frames) lift alternate warp threads while the weaver passes the shuttle carrying the weft thread back and forth.
- **Spinning wheel:** A large wooden spinning wheel in one corner for spinning raw wool or flax into thread
- **Winding equipment:** A swift (umbrella-shaped yarn holder) and a niddy-noddy for skeining yarn
- **Dyeing setup (if present):** A copper or iron vat on a brazier for heating dye baths. Small pots of dyestuff: crushed madder root (red), woad leaves (blue), weld (yellow), walnut hulls (brown), iron mordant (black)
- **Drying lines:** Cords strung across the room with dyed yarn or freshly woven cloth draped to dry

### Textures and Colors
- The most colorful shop in the settlement
- Bolts of cloth in deep madder red, woad blue, weld yellow, Lincoln green, natural cream wool, brown frieze cloth, pale flax linen
- Thread in vivid colors wound on wooden spools
- Fabric scraps on the floor in a rainbow of hues
- The tailor's tools are polished from constant handling -- bright iron scissors, smooth brass thimble

### Lighting
- **Ground floor:** Good light needed for detail work -- large windows, the counter-shutter open, and candles for evening work. The tailor positions the cutting table near the best light.
- **Loom room:** Needs less precision light -- but the weaver needs to see thread colors clearly, so windows along the loom side are important

### Atmosphere/Smell Cues
- Clean, dry textiles: wool has a distinctive lanolin smell (slightly oily, animal); linen smells of dried flax (grassy, slightly musty)
- Dye chemicals: woad smells faintly of ammonia; madder has an earthy, root-like scent; mordant baths (alum, iron) have a mineral tang
- Beeswax from the thread (waxed for strength)
- The rhythmic click-clack of the loom's heddles and shuttle is the dominant sound

## 2.9 Carpenter Workshop

### Purpose
Produces wooden goods ranging from furniture and building components to barrels, carts, and tools. The carpenter is distinct from the joiner (fine furniture), the cooper (barrels), and the wheelwright (wheels/carts), but in smaller settlements, one carpenter does all.

### Exterior Appearance
- **Structure:** A large, barn-like timber building, open-fronted or with wide doors. The front yard is stacked with timber in various stages of seasoning.
- **Timber yard:** Stacks of planks, beams, and logs leaning against walls or stacked on trestles. Green (fresh) wood seasoning in the air. Shavings and sawdust pile up outside the door.
- **Saw pit:** In the yard, a deep rectangular pit (8 feet long, 4 feet wide, 4 feet deep) where one man stands below and another above, operating a two-man pit saw to rip logs into planks.

### Interior

**Workshop:**
- **Floor:** Packed earth or rough stone, covered in a permanent layer of wood shavings, sawdust, and wood chips -- soft and springy underfoot
- **Workbench:** A massive oak workbench (8-10 feet long, 3 feet wide, 3 feet high) with a thick top, a woodworking vise (wooden screw mechanism) at one end, and numerous holes (dog holes) for bench dogs (wooden pegs that hold pieces in place)
- **Tool rack (wall-mounted):**
  - Hand saws (crosscut saw, rip saw, bow saw, coping saw)
  - Planes (smoothing plane, jack plane, jointing plane) with wooden bodies and iron blades
  - Chisels (mortise chisels, paring chisels, gouges) in a wooden rack, sorted by size
  - Mallet (wooden) and hammer (iron)
  - Augers (hand drills) and gimlets for boring holes
  - A drawknife (two-handled blade for shaping)
  - An adze (axe-like tool for rough shaping)
  - Marking gauge, brace-and-bit, wooden squares, a plumb bob
- **Shaving horse:** A foot-operated clamping bench where the carpenter sits and uses a drawknife to shape spindles, handles, and round stock
- **Lathe (foot-operated):** A pole lathe or spring-pole lathe for turning chair legs, bowls, and other cylindrical items. A leather cord wraps around the workpiece; a foot treadle spins it back and forth while the turner holds a chisel to it.
- **Assembly area:** A clear floor space for assembling larger pieces (doors, furniture, carts)
- **Glue pot:** A small iron pot on a brazier, heating animal-hide glue (smells strongly of cooked bones)
- **Lumber storage (rear):** Racks holding planks sorted by species (oak, elm, ash, beech, pine) and thickness. The wood is air-dried for months to years before use.

### Textures and Colors
- **Wood species:** Pale golden oak, creamy-white ash, warm brown elm, pinkish beech, pale yellow pine -- each visible in shavings and cut surfaces
- **Shavings:** Thin, curled shavings everywhere in pale cream and gold
- **Tools:** Bright steel blades, smooth wooden handles darkened with hand oil
- **Finished pieces:** Smooth, planed surfaces showing grain patterns -- some pieces are being stained or oiled

### Lighting
- Open front or large doors for maximum daylight -- carpenters need to see grain and tool marks clearly
- In winter or evening: candles in lanterns (open flame dangerous with sawdust, so enclosed lanterns only)

### Atmosphere/Smell Cues
- Fresh-cut wood: the dominant scent -- each species has its own character (oak is tannic, pine is resinous, cedar is aromatic)
- Sawdust: dry, clean, slightly sweet
- Animal-hide glue: warm, meaty, slightly unpleasant when heating
- Linseed oil (for finishing): a nutty, organic smell
- The sounds: the rhythmic rasp of a saw, the tap of mallet on chisel, the swish of a plane, the creak of the lathe

## 2.10 Jeweler/Goldsmith

### Purpose
Works precious metals (gold, silver) and gemstones to create jewelry, ecclesiastical objects (chalices, reliquaries, crosses), signet rings, brooches, belt buckles, and decorative items. Among the highest-status craftsmen, often working for the nobility and clergy. In a dark fantasy setting, the goldsmith may also create enchanted items, inscribe protective runes, or set magical gemstones.

### Exterior Appearance
- **Structure:** A secure stone-built shop (ground floor) with timber upper story for living. The ground floor has thick walls and a heavy lockable iron-bound door -- security is paramount.
- **Windows:** Small, with iron bars or heavy shutters (robbery prevention). Finished pieces displayed behind the barred window on a velvet cloth.
- **Sign:** A golden or gilded ring, or a hand holding a gem, hung from an iron bracket.
- **Security:** A heavy iron lock on the door; possibly a guard dog.

### Interior

**Workshop:**
- **Floor:** Clean flagstone -- precious metal filings and gemstone dust are far too valuable to lose in a dirt floor. A skin apron is spread beneath the workbench to catch every particle.
- **Workbench:** A heavy, sturdy bench at a window for natural light. The bench has a semi-circular cutout (the "bench pin") projecting over the edge, against which the goldsmith braces work. On the bench:
  - Small hammers (chasing hammers, planishing hammers) with polished faces
  - Gravers and burins (sharp steel points for engraving)
  - Files (needle files in many profiles: flat, half-round, triangular, square)
  - Tweezers and small pliers (flat-nose, round-nose)
  - A jeweler's saw (a fine-toothed blade in a small frame)
  - Magnifying lens (a polished crystal or early glass lens mounted on a stand or worn as a loupe)
  - A small anvil (stake) mounted in a wooden block
  - Punches and dies for stamping decorative patterns
  - A burnisher (smooth steel or agate tool for polishing metal)
- **Furnace/Forge:** A small charcoal-fueled furnace or cupellation hearth for melting and refining metals. Much smaller than a blacksmith's forge but hotter (gold melts at 1064C). A small bellows feeds air.
  - Crucibles (small ceramic vessels) for melting gold and silver
  - Ingot molds (stone or iron)
  - Tongs and pouring tools
  - A cupel (bone-ash cup) for assaying (testing) metal purity
- **Soldering station:** A charcoal block or magnesia block for soldering small pieces with a mouth-blown blowpipe directing a flame
- **Gem-setting area:** A shellac-covered wooden block (a "setter's cement") holds a piece while gemstones are set. Small gemstones in folded paper packets. A gem-holder (wooden dop stick with shellac) for polishing stones.
- **Polishing equipment:** A lathe with a spinning polishing wheel, or hand-polishing with rouge (iron oxide) on a cloth
- **Touchstone:** A smooth black stone (slate or jasper) for testing metal purity by rubbing the metal against it and comparing the streak color to known standards

**Strong room / Vault:**
- A locked iron-bound chest or a stone-walled closet with an iron door
- Contains raw gold and silver bars/ingots, unset gemstones, finished high-value pieces, client deposits
- A ledger records every grain of precious metal

**Display Area:**
- A small section of the front room with a locked glass-fronted cabinet or a velvet-lined display tray behind the barred window
- Finished rings, brooches, pendants, chains, and ecclesiastical objects
- Only shown to trusted clients

### Textures and Colors
- **Metals:** Gleaming yellow gold, bright white silver, warm rose-gold, dull red copper (for alloys). Metal shavings and dust glint in the light.
- **Gemstones:** Deep red garnet, blue sapphire, green emerald, purple amethyst, clear rock crystal, pearls -- points of concentrated color
- **Tools:** Highly polished steel, smooth hardwood handles
- **Workshop:** Clean and precise -- a contrast to the rougher craft shops. Every tool has its place.
- **Charcoal:** Black, with a faint reddish glow when the furnace is lit

### Lighting
- **Critical:** The goldsmith needs the best possible natural light -- the bench is placed at the window, facing north (for consistent, non-glaring light). A polished metal reflector may direct additional light onto the work.
- **Supplementary:** Beeswax candles (brighter and cleaner than tallow) in a multi-arm candelabra focused on the workbench
- **The furnace:** Provides a warm orange glow in the rear of the shop
- **Overall:** Bright at the bench, dim elsewhere

### Atmosphere/Smell Cues
- Hot metal and charcoal from the furnace
- The faint chemical smell of flux (borax) used in soldering
- Clean mineral scent of gemstone dust
- Beeswax from candles and the shellac cement
- Leather from the apron
- An atmosphere of precision and quiet concentration -- none of the noise and soot of the blacksmith

## 3.1 Village Chapel

### Purpose
The spiritual center of the village -- serves a congregation of 50-200 people. A parish priest (often barely literate himself) conducts mass, baptisms, marriages, and funerals. The building also serves as a community meeting place, a safe-haven during raids, and the only stone building in many villages.

### Exterior Appearance
- **Structure:** Simple stone construction (local rubble stone or fieldstone, lime-mortared). Rectangular nave with a smaller square or apsidal chancel. No transept.
- **Roof:** Steep-pitched, covered in stone slates, clay tiles, or in poorer areas, thatch. A small wooden bell-cote or stone bellcote at the west gable, housing a single iron bell.
- **Tower (if present):** A squat square tower, barely rising above the roofline, at the west end
- **Windows:** Narrow lancet windows (pointed arch, 6-12 inches wide) with no glass or simple clear glass. One small window in the chancel may have painted glass.
- **Door:** A heavy oak door in a simple rounded or pointed stone arch at the west end. Iron strap hinges, a large iron ring handle, and a wooden latch. The door arch may have simple carved chevron or beak-head moulding.
- **Porch:** A small stone or timber porch sheltering the door -- business and legal transactions were conducted "at the church door."
- **Churchyard:** A low stone wall encloses a graveyard with simple wooden crosses or rough stone markers. An ancient yew tree. A lych-gate (roofed gate) where coffins rested before burial.
- **Size:** 40-60 feet long, 15-20 feet wide. The chancel is smaller: 15 feet square.

### Interior

**Nave:**
- **Floor:** Beaten earth or rough flagstone, uneven and worn. In later periods, some encaustic tiles near the altar.
- **No pews:** The congregation stands (pews are a late medieval addition). Possibly a few stone benches along the walls for the elderly and infirm ("the weakest go to the wall").
- **Walls:** Lime-plastered and covered with painted murals (wall paintings). Common subjects:
  - A "Doom" painting above the chancel arch -- the Last Judgment with Christ enthroned, the saved ascending to heaven (left), and the damned dragged to a gaping hellmouth (right)
  - St. Christopher (giant carrying the Christ child) on the north wall, visible to all who enter
  - The Seven Deadly Sins, scenes from the life of Christ, patron saint of the church
  - The paintings are done in earth pigments: red ochre, yellow ochre, lime white, charcoal black, and verdigris green
- **Font:** A large stone baptismal font near the door (west end) -- cylindrical or octagonal, carved with simple arcading or figures, with a locked wooden cover (to prevent theft of holy water for witchcraft)
- **Rood screen:** A wooden screen separating the nave from the chancel, topped with a large painted wooden crucifix (the "rood") flanked by figures of the Virgin Mary and St. John. The screen has tracery openings and may be brightly painted in red, blue, green, and gold.
- **Pulpit (if present):** A simple wooden or stone platform against the north wall of the nave
- **Poor box:** A locked wooden box chained to a pillar for alms
- **Holy water stoup:** A stone basin near the door, filled with blessed water

**Chancel:**
- **Altar:** A simple stone altar table (the mensa) covered with a white linen cloth, with a silk or embroidered frontal hanging in front
- **On the altar:** Two brass or pewter candlesticks, a brass cross, and sometimes a painted wooden triptych (reredos) depicting saints
- **Piscina:** A stone niche with a drain, for washing sacred vessels
- **Aumbry:** A small recess in the wall (sometimes with a wooden door) for storing the communion plate and reserved sacrament
- **Sedilia:** Stone seats built into the south wall for the priest and assistants during mass
- **East window:** The finest window -- may have some painted glass with a simple figure of the patron saint

### Textures and Colors
- **Stone:** Grey-brown rubble stone with cream lime mortar
- **Plaster:** Warm cream-white background for the vivid wall paintings
- **Wall paintings:** Red ochre, yellow ochre, black, white, and green -- somewhat faded, with areas of flaking and water damage
- **Wood:** The rood screen is the most colorful element -- bright reds, blues, greens, and gilt on carved tracery
- **Floor:** Dark grey stone, worn smooth in the path from door to altar

### Lighting
- **Dim and atmospheric:** The narrow lancet windows admit limited light. The interior is perpetually in soft shadow.
- **Candles:** Two altar candles provide warm yellow light at the east end. During services, additional candles may be lit in wall sconces.
- **The Doom painting:** Looms above in the semi-darkness, the figures half-seen -- deliberately intimidating

### Atmosphere/Smell Cues
- Cold stone: the chapel is always cool, even in summer
- Damp plaster: a slight musty smell, especially after rain
- Incense (frankincense): lingers from the last mass, a sweet, resinous scent
- Beeswax candles: clean, sweet, warm
- Old wood of the rood screen
- Earth from the floor
- The faint scent of wildflowers placed as offerings at the altar

## 3.2 Cathedral/Abbey

### Purpose
The seat of a bishop (cathedral) or the church of a major monastic community (abbey). A statement of divine power and civic pride, designed to awe and inspire. Took decades or centuries to build. Functions as a place of worship, pilgrimage destination, administrative center, school, and occasionally a fortress.

### Exterior Appearance
- **Construction:** Dressed stone (ashlar) -- limestone, sandstone, or marble. Flying buttresses support the tall walls of the nave, transferring the outward thrust of the stone vaulting to external supports. Pinnacles atop the buttresses add weight.
- **West front (main facade):** Two flanking towers (100-300 feet tall) with a central portal between them. The portal has deeply recessed archways (archivolts) with carved figures (prophets, saints, angels) in the jambs and tympanum (the semi-circular panel above the door showing the Last Judgment or Christ in Majesty). A large rose window above the portal -- stone tracery holding stained glass 20-30 feet in diameter.
- **Roof:** Steep lead-covered roof behind stone parapets. A central spire or tower at the crossing (where nave meets transept), sometimes reaching 400+ feet.
- **Gargoyles:** Carved stone water spouts projecting from the walls -- grotesque animal or demon figures that channel rainwater away from the walls
- **Flying buttresses:** Visible along the sides, arching from the upper walls to freestanding piers -- a defining Gothic feature
- **Scale:** The nave may be 300-500 feet long, 100-150 feet wide (including aisles), with a vault height of 80-130 feet

### Interior

**The Nave:**
- **Scale:** A vast, soaring interior designed to make humans feel small before God. The pointed ribbed vaults draw the eye upward. The ceiling may be 100+ feet above.
- **Elevation (three levels):**
  1. Ground-level arcade: tall pointed arches on massive clustered columns separating the nave from the side aisles
  2. Triforium: a narrow arcaded gallery above the arcade, running the length of the nave
  3. Clerestory: the highest level, with large stained glass windows flooding the upper space with colored light
- **Floor:** Encaustic tiles in geometric patterns (black, red, gold, green), or marble pavements. Tomb slabs of brass or stone set into the floor (memorial brasses with engraved figures of knights, bishops, and nobles).
- **Columns:** Massive stone piers (6-10 feet in diameter) of clustered shafts rising to the vault -- some carved with stiff-leaf foliage capitals
- **Wooden choir stalls:** Carved oak stalls in two rows facing each other in the choir (east end), with misericords (mercy seats -- hinged seats with carved undersides showing fantastical creatures, domestic scenes, or grotesques)
- **Pulpit:** An elaborate stone or wooden pulpit on the nave's north side, with carved panels and a sounding board above
- **Stone or wood rood screen:** Separating the nave (public) from the choir (clergy) -- elaborately carved with saints, foliage, and tracery, often surmounted by a crucifix
- **Side chapels:** Small chapels opening off the aisles, each with its own altar, patron saint statue, and candle stands. Wealthy families endow chantry chapels for prayers for the dead.

**The Transept:**
- Cross-arms of the building, each with its own large stained glass window (north and south transepts)
- Additional side altars and chapels
- A central crossing marked by massive piers supporting the central tower

**The Chancel/Choir:**
- The liturgical heart -- where the clergy conduct daily services (8 times per day)
- Elaborately carved stone or wooden choir screen enclosing the space
- The high altar: a massive stone table with rich embroidered frontals, a jeweled altar cross, gold or silver candlesticks, a tabernacle for the reserved sacrament
- A brass eagle lectern for reading scripture
- A bishop's throne (cathedra) -- an elaborate carved stone or wooden chair, often raised on steps

**The Ambulatory:**
- A walkway curving around the east end of the chancel behind the high altar
- Pilgrim access to shrine chapels without disrupting services
- The chapel of the patron saint, containing relics in a jeweled reliquary (gold and silver casket, studded with gems, visible through crystal panels)

**The Crypt:**
- Below the chancel, a low-vaulted stone chamber
- Tombs of bishops and nobles -- stone sarcophagi with carved recumbent effigies
- A secondary altar
- Dark, cool, and echoing

**The Cloister (if abbey):**
- See Monastery section (3.3) below

### Textures and Colors
- **Stone:** Pale golden limestone or cream sandstone, clean-cut with precise jointing. Some surfaces darkened with soot from centuries of candles.
- **Stained glass:** The dominant color source -- deep ruby red, sapphire blue, emerald green, amethyst purple, golden yellow. The light shifts through the day as the sun moves.
- **Floor tiles:** Rich patterns in terracotta red, golden yellow, olive green, and black
- **Metalwork:** Gilded bronze screens, polished brass lecterns, wrought iron candle stands
- **Wood:** Dark oak choir stalls with a deep, rich patina from centuries of beeswax polishing
- **Painted surfaces:** Vault bosses and capitals painted in vivid colors with gold leaf highlights

### Lighting
- **Stained glass light:** The defining quality -- shafts of colored light move across the interior as the sun tracks across the sky, painting the stone with pools of ruby, sapphire, and gold. The effect is deliberately transcendent.
- **Candles:** Hundreds of candles on altar, in chandeliers (wheel-chandeliers), in wall sconces, and at side chapel altars. The aggregate candlelight creates a warm golden haze.
- **Contrast:** The upper clerestory is brilliantly lit by the stained glass; the lower arcades and aisles are in deep shadow. Side chapels glow with individual candle clusters.
- **Night:** Almost entirely dark except for the sanctuary lamp (a perpetual flame near the tabernacle) and the sexton's lantern

### Atmosphere/Smell Cues
- Incense (frankincense and myrrh): thick and ever-present, lingering in the stonework
- Beeswax candles: clean and sweet
- Cold stone: a permanent chill, even in summer; the temperature drops noticeably on entering
- Damp stone: in the crypt and lower walls, a musty, mineral smell
- Old wood and beeswax polish from the choir stalls
- The faint metallic scent of brass and bronze
- Silence broken by echoing footsteps, the distant chant of the choir, the creak of wooden stalls
- The psychological atmosphere of immense vertical space -- awe, reverence, and a slight unease at one's own smallness

## 3.3 Monastery

### Purpose
Self-contained religious community for monks or nuns following a monastic rule (Benedictine, Cistercian, Augustinian). A place of prayer, study, work, and contemplation. Also functions as a school, hospital, hostelry, farm, brewery, and center of learning and manuscript production.

### Exterior Appearance
- **Layout:** Buildings arranged around a central cloister (covered walkway enclosing a rectangular garden). The church forms the north side (for maximum sun in the cloister); the domestic buildings surround the other three sides.
- **Church:** Simpler than a cathedral but still substantial -- a cruciform stone building with a tower
- **Overall impression:** A walled compound, almost village-sized, with multiple stone buildings, gardens, orchards, fish ponds, and farm buildings

### Interior Rooms

**The Cloister:**
- A covered walkway with an open arcade (stone columns and pointed arches) surrounding a rectangular garden (the garth)
- The garth: a central well or fountain, herb beds in geometric patterns, a few fruit trees, gravel paths. Peaceful and symmetrical.
- The cloister walk: paved with stone flags, the inner wall (against the church) is solid; the outer wall is the open arcade. Monks walk and read here.
- Carved stone capitals on the arcade columns depicting biblical scenes, foliage, and sometimes humorous monsters
- A washing trough (lavatorium) in the cloister near the refectory entrance -- a long stone basin with a row of small lion's-head spouts, where monks wash their hands before meals

**The Chapter House:**
- East range, accessible from the cloister through an ornate doorway flanked by open arches (so monks in the cloister can hear)
- The room where the community meets daily to hear a chapter of the monastic rule, conduct business, and confess faults
- A vaulted stone room with a central column (or columns) supporting the vault
- Stone bench seating around the walls for the monks
- The abbot's seat (more elaborate) at the east end
- A lectern for reading
- A tile floor with geometric patterns

**The Scriptorium/Library:**
- Upper floor of the east range (for light) or a room with large windows facing north
- Long wooden desks (carrels) angled toward the windows, each with a writing slope
- At each desk: an inkwell (horn or ceramic), a pen-rest, quills (goose or swan feathers), a pounce pot (fine sand for drying ink), a penknife for trimming quills and scraping errors
- A large reference book (a Bible or patristic text) chained to a shared reading desk
- Shelving for completed manuscripts -- books lying flat, spine hidden, with identifying marks on the page edges
- In the library: long lecterns between the windows with chained books, a ladder for the upper shelves
- A warming pan (metal dish of coals) under the desk in winter -- the only concession to comfort
- NO candles (fire risk to irreplaceable manuscripts)

**The Refectory (Dining Hall):**
- South range, entered from the cloister
- A long hall with a high vaulted ceiling
- Two long trestle tables running the length of the room with bench seating
- The abbot's table on a slight dais at the head
- A pulpit (reading desk) built into one wall, accessed by narrow stairs -- a monk reads aloud from scripture or the church fathers during the silent meal
- Ceramic bowls, wooden spoons, simple pottery cups -- identical for all
- A serving hatch connecting to the kitchen
- A large crucifix on the wall above the abbot's table
- Lime-plastered walls, possibly with a painted Last Supper scene

**The Dormitory (Dorter):**
- Upper floor of the east range
- A long open room with a row of straw mattresses on low wooden platforms (later divided into individual cubicles by curtains or wooden partitions)
- Night stairs: a staircase connecting directly to the church transept for night-time services (the monks are woken at 2 AM for Matins)
- A single candle at each end of the room (supervision, not comfort)
- Each monk's space: a palliasse, a single blanket, a wooden peg for their habit

**The Calefactory (Warming Room):**
- The only heated room besides the kitchen
- A large fireplace or hypocaust (underfloor heating in Cistercian houses)
- Monks sit here to warm themselves in winter, do hand-work (mending), or relax during allowed periods
- Benches and a few stools around the fire
- A cat (monastery cats were common for mice control)

**The Kitchen:**
- A large, high-ceilinged room with a massive fireplace or central hearth
- Monastic diet was largely vegetarian (fish on feast days): large cauldrons for soups and stews, bread ovens, a fish-preparation table
- Brewing equipment: monasteries produced their own ale and sometimes wine
- A herb garden accessible through a door

**The Infirmary:**
- A separate building for sick monks, with a small chapel attached
- Beds with linen sheets (better than the dormitory)
- A dispensary with herbs and medicines
- A fireplace for warmth

**The Reredorter (Necessarium/Toilets):**
- Connected to the dormitory, extending over a water channel
- A row of stone or wooden seats over a drain flushed by diverted stream water
- The most sophisticated sanitation in the medieval world

### Textures and Colors
- **Stone:** Pale, clean ashlar -- Cistercian houses deliberately austere (no painted decoration)
- **Cloister garden:** Green herbs and grass, grey stone, white gravel paths
- **Scriptorium:** Pale parchment, black and red ink, colorful illuminated capitals (gold leaf, lapis lazuli blue, vermillion red)
- **Refectory:** Dark oak tables, white lime plaster, a painted crucifix
- **Overall:** An aesthetic of deliberate simplicity -- clean stone, minimal decoration, functionality

### Lighting
- **Cloister:** Open to the sky -- bright daylight in the garth, deep shade in the covered walks
- **Scriptorium:** Maximized natural light through large windows -- manuscripts cannot be copied by candlelight
- **Church:** Depending on the order: Cistercian churches have clear glass (bright, austere); Benedictine churches may have stained glass (colored light)
- **Dormitory:** Near darkness at night; grey dawn light through small windows

### Atmosphere/Smell Cues
- **Silence:** The dominant "sound" -- monks observe silence except during specified times. Footsteps echo on stone. The scratch of quills in the scriptorium. Distant chanting from the church at the hours.
- Incense from the church drifts through the cloister
- Herbs from the garden: lavender, rosemary, thyme, mint
- Baking bread from the kitchen
- Damp stone and lime plaster
- Parchment and ink in the scriptorium (a dry, slightly animal smell from the vellum)
- The calefactory: wood smoke and the warmth of bodies crowding near the fire
- A pervasive sense of order, regularity, and peace -- or in a dark fantasy context, an oppressive, cloistered tension hiding something beneath the surface

## 3.4 Graveyard Chapel / Ossuary

### Purpose
A charnel house or ossuary stores exhumed human bones when graveyard space is reclaimed. A chapel above (or adjacent to) the bone storage provides a space for prayers for the dead. These became common from the 13th century, linked to beliefs about Purgatory and the importance of praying for departed souls. In dark fantasy: a nexus of necromantic energy, a place where the veil between life and death is thin.

### Exterior Appearance
- **Structure:** A small stone building, often semi-subterranean. The lower level (the ossuary) may have its wall partially below ground, with small windows high in the wall allowing the bones to be seen from outside. The upper level (the chapel) is at ground level or slightly above.
- **Size:** 20-30 feet long, 15-20 feet wide, single story or story-and-a-half
- **Walls:** Rough stone or flint, lime-mortared. The stone is dark with age and often green with damp moss.
- **Roof:** Stone slate or tile, low-pitched
- **Windows:** Small, barred, at the ossuary level. The chapel has slightly larger lancet windows.
- **Door:** A heavy oak door with iron reinforcement. A carved skull or cross above the lintel.
- **Location:** Within the churchyard, near the church. Steps lead down to the ossuary entrance.
- **Atmosphere:** The building feels older and more neglected than the church. Weeds grow between the stones. The ground around it is uneven from centuries of digging.

### Interior

**The Ossuary (Lower Level):**
- **Access:** Stone steps descending through a low arched doorway
- **Ceiling:** Low barrel-vault or groin-vault, 6-7 feet high. The stone is damp and bears mineral deposits (white calcium staining).
- **Floor:** Flagstone, uneven, often damp
- **The bones:** Human skeletal remains arranged along the walls:
  - Skulls stacked in rows, some in neat lines, some in pyramidal arrangements
  - Long bones (femurs, tibias) stacked horizontally in patterns -- sometimes in a herringbone or checkerboard arrangement with skulls
  - In more elaborate ossuaries: bones arranged decoratively -- arches of femurs, rosettes of skulls, columns of pelvises
  - Some bones are darkened with age (brown, yellow-brown); others are pale cream-white
  - Scattered smaller bones (vertebrae, ribs, finger bones) fill the gaps
- **Quantity:** Thousands of individuals in a parish ossuary -- the bones may fill the room floor-to-ceiling along every wall
- **Furniture:** None in the ossuary itself -- it is purely storage. A single iron candle sconce on the wall. Perhaps a stone shelf for a lantern.
- **Dark fantasy additions:** One skull has faintly glowing eye sockets. Scratch marks on the inside of a stone slab. A whispered voice at the edge of hearing. Bone dust glitters faintly in the lantern light.

**The Chapel (Upper Level):**
- A small, simple room with a stone altar at the east end
- The altar has a plain linen cloth, a brass crucifix, and two candles
- A painted mural of the Danse Macabre (Dance of Death) -- skeletons leading people of all social classes (king, pope, merchant, peasant) in a procession toward the grave
- A wooden pew or two
- A poor box for donations
- A small stained glass window depicting the Resurrection or the Last Judgment
- A chantry priest's vestment chest
- An iron-bound door leading down to the ossuary

### Textures and Colors
- **Bone:** Cream, ivory, yellow-brown, dark brown -- centuries of patina. Some bones are smooth; others have a rough, powdery surface.
- **Stone:** Dark grey to black with damp, white mineral streaks, green moss near the windows
- **Chapel walls:** Lime-washed white, but the Danse Macabre painting uses stark black skeletons against a pale background
- **Air:** Visible moisture in the ossuary; dust motes in the chapel's shafts of window light

### Lighting
- **Ossuary:** Near total darkness. A single candle or lantern creates a pool of warm light that makes the skull stacks seem to watch the visitor. Shadows shift and jump.
- **Chapel:** Dim daylight through small windows. Candlelight at the altar. The Danse Macabre figures emerge from and retreat into shadow.

### Atmosphere/Smell Cues
- **Ossuary:** Dry bone dust (a specific, mineral, chalk-like smell), damp stone, stale air. The silence is absolute. The temperature is cold and constant. A faint organic undertone from very old remains.
- **Chapel:** Cold stone, incense residue, candle wax, musty air
- **Dark fantasy:** An oppressive heaviness in the air. The feeling of being watched. A faint susurrus like distant breathing. The candle flame flickers without a draft.

## 3.5 Shrine / Wayside Shrine

### Purpose
A small devotional structure at a crossroads, town boundary, or significant location (the site of a miracle, a death, or a sacred spring). Travelers stop to pray for safe passage. Offerings of candles, flowers, coins, and small tokens are left. Some shrines house a relic or a locally venerated holy image.

### Exterior Appearance -- Full Shrine
- **Structure:** A small stone or timber structure, 4-8 feet tall, 3-5 feet wide. Resembles a tiny chapel or a large niche.
- **Design variations:**
  - **Column shrine:** A stone pillar 6-8 feet tall, topped with a carved saint's figure or a lantern niche
  - **Niche shrine:** A stone wall alcove with a pointed arch, containing a painted wooden or stone figure of a saint
  - **Chapel shrine:** A miniature building (too small to enter) with a tiny door or open front, containing a figurine and a small altar
  - **Crucifix shrine:** A tall wooden or stone cross (8-12 feet) sheltered by a small timber roof, often with a carved corpus (body of Christ) -- common in Alpine regions with elaborate wood carving
- **Materials:** Local stone (rubble or dressed), timber, sometimes brick. Roofed with slate, tile, or a timber shingle cap.
- **Offerings around the base:** Dried and fresh flowers, small candles (some burned down to stubs), a few coins, a length of ribbon, a crudely carved wooden figure (a votive offering)
- **Condition:** Varies from well-tended (bright flowers, fresh whitewash) to neglected (crumbling, overgrown with ivy, the saint's face weathered smooth)

### Interior (of niche or chapel-type shrine)
- A small painted wooden or stone statue of a saint (12-18 inches tall). Common subjects: the Virgin Mary, St. Christopher, the local patron saint
- A tiny altar shelf with:
  - 1-2 candle stubs in iron or ceramic holders
  - A small brass or tin cross
  - A votive offering (a wax figure representing a healed limb, a lock of hair, a small coin purse)
  - Dried flowers in a small clay pot
- The interior may be painted with stars on a blue background (heaven) or with a simple floral border
- A small kneeling stone or wooden platform before the shrine for prayer

### Textures and Colors
- Weathered stone: grey with white lichen and green moss
- Painted figures: faded blues, reds, and golds -- the paint cracking and peeling with age
- Timber: silvered grey from weathering
- Offerings: the only bright colors -- fresh flowers in season

### Lighting
- Open to the sky -- natural daylight
- At night or in the shrine niche: the warm glow of a single votive candle, flickering in the wind, casting moving shadows on the saint's face

### Atmosphere/Smell Cues
- Outdoors: wind, birdsong, the creak of the timber roof
- Candle wax (beeswax or tallow)
- Wildflowers
- Damp stone and moss
- A sense of quiet reverence, of many generations of prayers soaked into the stone
- Dark fantasy: a crossroads shrine may be a place of power -- the veil is thin where paths cross. The saint's painted eyes seem to follow the traveler. The candle won't stay lit in one corner. Local folk leave extra offerings here, and not all are pious.

# MILITARY

## 4.1 Guard Barracks

### Purpose
Houses the town guard or garrison soldiers. A functional, no-frills building designed for sleeping, eating, and storing equipment. Accommodates 10-50 soldiers depending on the settlement size.

### Exterior Appearance
- **Structure:** A long rectangular stone or half-timber building, usually within or adjacent to the town walls or keep. Two stories: ground floor common areas, upper floor dormitory.
- **Appearance:** Utilitarian -- no decoration. Thick walls, small windows (for defense), a single sturdy door.
- **Yard:** A training yard with a weapon rack, archery butts (straw-filled targets), and a well.

### Interior Rooms

**Ground Floor -- Common Room/Mess Hall:**
- Long trestle tables and benches for meals
- A large stone fireplace at one end
- Iron pot hooks and a cooking cauldron for the common pot
- A barrel of ale and a stack of wooden cups
- A dice table in one corner with stools
- Tallow candles in iron wall sconces
- A notice board with patrol rosters and duty assignments
- A punishment post (for minor infractions)

**Ground Floor -- Duty Room:**
- A desk or table with the watch commander's log (a leather-bound book)
- Quill, ink, and sand-shaker
- A rack of keys (for the gates and cells)
- A map of the town and surrounding area (crude, hand-drawn on vellum, pinned to the wall)
- A lantern and a horn (for sounding the alarm)

**Upper Floor -- Dormitory:**
- Rows of narrow wooden cot frames with straw mattresses, 2-3 feet apart
- A rough wool blanket and a straw-filled pillow per bed
- A wooden footlocker at the end of each bed for personal items
- Wall pegs above each bed for hanging armor, a sword belt, and a cloak
- A communal wash basin (ceramic or wooden) on a stand with a water pitcher
- A single privy at one end (a wooden seat over a shaft)
- A brazier or small fireplace for winter warmth

**Armory Closet (ground floor, locked):**
- A heavy iron-bound door
- Wall-mounted racks holding spare weapons: spears, halberds, crossbows
- Shelves with spare crossbow bolts, bowstrings, whetstones
- A few sets of chain mail and leather armor on wooden stands
- Shield racks along one wall

### Textures and Colors
- **Stone:** Grey and soot-stained. Utilitarian.
- **Wood:** Rough-sawn, unfinished, scarred from use
- **Metal:** Functional black iron -- pot hooks, candle holders, weapon racks
- **Bedding:** Brown and grey wool, straw yellow
- **Overall:** Drab, functional, worn -- everything shows heavy use

### Lighting
- Dim -- small windows, tallow candles, firelight. The dormitory is dark at night except for a single candle by the stairs.

### Atmosphere/Smell Cues
- Sweat and leather (from armor and gear)
- Stale ale and wood smoke
- Damp wool and straw from the beds
- Metal and oil (from weapon maintenance)
- The sour smell of men living in close quarters
- Boot leather and mud
- Sound: snoring, the clink of dice, the creak of the cot frames, distant watch-bell

## 4.2 Armory

### Purpose
Dedicated storage for weapons, armor, and military equipment. Distinct from a barracks armory closet -- this is a larger, purpose-built room or building for a castle or wealthy town's military stores. Maintained by an armorer or quartermaster.

### Exterior Appearance
- **Structure:** A stone-built room or building within a castle, keep, or town wall complex. Thick walls, a heavy iron-bound door with a serious lock. Small or no windows (security).
- **Size:** 20-40 feet long, 15-20 feet wide

### Interior

- **Floor:** Stone flags, dry and clean
- **Wall racks (most of the wall space):**
  - Sword racks: vertical wooden racks holding 10-30 swords (longswords, arming swords, falchions) in leather scabbards
  - Polearm racks: halberds, spears, pikes, bills, and glaives standing vertically in wooden frames
  - Crossbow racks: crossbows hung from wall pegs, with quivers of bolts below
  - Bow racks: longbows standing vertically, with spare bowstrings in oiled-cloth pouches
- **Armor stands:** Wooden torso forms (like dress forms) holding:
  - Chain mail hauberks
  - Brigandine vests (leather with riveted plates)
  - Plate armor pieces (gauntlets, greaves, breastplates) in later periods
  - Helmets (bascinets, kettle hats, great helms) on shelf pegs
- **Shield rack:** Large and small shields leaning against a wall rack: kite shields, heater shields, bucklers
- **Maintenance bench:** A long table with:
  - Whetstones and oil for sharpening
  - Leather strips and rivets for repairs
  - Chain mail rings and pliers for mending mail
  - A small forge (or brazier) for heating rivets
  - A vise for holding blades during sharpening
  - Oil and wax rags for preventing rust
- **Storage chests:** Iron-bound chests containing:
  - Arrow heads and crossbow bolt heads (sorted by type)
  - Spare leather straps, buckles, and hardware
  - Siege equipment components (grappling hooks, rope coils)
- **Inventory:** A ledger on a stand, recording every item, its condition, and who has signed it out

### Textures and Colors
- **Metal:** The dominant material -- polished steel (bright grey), oiled iron (dark grey with a slight sheen), corroded iron (reddish-brown rust patches on neglected pieces), blued steel (dark blue-black)
- **Leather:** Dark brown (scabbards, armor straps), sometimes dyed black
- **Wood:** Oak and ash (weapon shafts, racks) in warm brown tones
- **Heraldry:** Shield faces painted with bright heraldic colors -- gules (red), azure (blue), or (gold), sable (black)

### Lighting
- Dim -- deliberately. Only carried lanterns and a few wall sconces. Windows are absent or minimal for security.
- Metallic reflections glint in the lantern light -- an eerie effect with rows of helmets and blade edges catching the light

### Atmosphere/Smell Cues
- Metal and oil: the dominant scent -- cold iron, linseed oil, and wax used to protect steel from rust
- Leather: the rich, tannic smell of treated hides
- Dry stone and dust
- A slight chemical tang from the bluing process
- The room has a cold, purposeful stillness -- a promise of violence held in suspension

## 4.3 Watch Tower

### Purpose
An elevated observation post for spotting approaching threats (enemies, fires, storms) and signaling to the garrison. Usually built into the town wall circuit or standing free at a strategic vantage point.

### Exterior Appearance
- **Structure:** A tall, narrow stone tower, round or square, 30-60 feet high, 10-15 feet in diameter. Built of the same stone as the town walls, often with a slightly wider base tapering upward.
- **Top:** A crenellated parapet (alternating merlons and crenels) for defense. A wooden or stone beacon platform at the top for signal fires. A flagpole or banner pole.
- **Access:** A single narrow doorway at ground level (or sometimes accessed only from the wall-walk, with no ground-level entry)
- **Arrow loops:** Narrow vertical slits in the walls at various levels for archers

### Interior

**Ground Level:**
- A small stone room used for storage: firewood for the beacon, a barrel of pitch, rope, spare weapons
- The base of a spiral stone staircase (clockwise ascending -- designed so a right-handed defender can swing a sword more easily while ascending attackers are hindered)
- A stone floor, often damp

**Mid-Levels (1-2 intermediate floors):**
- Connected by the spiral stair
- A guard room: a simple table, a stool, a brazier for warmth, a candle lantern
- A straw pallet for the off-duty watchman to nap
- Arrow loops in the thick walls -- the stone embrasure is played outward inside to allow the archer a wider field of fire
- A wooden trap door in the floor that can be barred from above

**Top Platform:**
- Open to the sky, surrounded by the crenellated parapet
- A large iron fire-basket (cresset) on a stone or iron pedestal for the beacon fire, pre-loaded with pitch-soaked wood
- A horn or bell for sounding the alarm
- A spyglass or simply the watchman's sharp eyes
- A stone bench or seat
- A water bucket (for drinking and fire safety)
- A privy shaft (a projecting stone corbel with a hole, discharging down the outside of the tower)

### Textures and Colors
- Rough grey stone throughout, darkened by soot at the top (from beacon fires)
- Iron hardware: the cresset basket, door hinges, arrow-loop shutters
- Cold, exposed stonework worn smooth on the stair treads

### Lighting
- Ground and mid-levels: very dark -- only arrow loops and a carried candle
- Top: open sky -- brilliant daylight, wind, and weather

### Atmosphere/Smell Cues
- Cold stone, drafts through arrow loops, the whistle of wind at height
- Pitch and soot from the beacon basket
- The watchman's sweat, bread crusts, and ale remnants
- At the top: wide-open sky, the wind, and a commanding view of the surrounding landscape
- A sense of isolation and vigilance

## 4.4 Gatehouse

### Purpose
The primary controlled entry point through the town or castle walls. Combines defensive architecture with an administrative function (collecting tolls, checking visitors, controlling access). The most heavily fortified point in the defensive circuit.

### Exterior Appearance
- **Structure:** Two flanking towers (round or D-shaped) on either side of a vaulted passage, connected by a room above the passage. The towers rise 30-50 feet, the passage between them is 10-15 feet wide and 20-30 feet deep.
- **Gates:** A pair of massive oak gates at the outer end (6-8 inches thick, reinforced with iron plates and studs). The gates have a wicket door (a smaller door within the larger gate for pedestrian access when the main gates are closed).
- **Portcullis:** An iron-and-oak grid gate that slides vertically in stone grooves. Some gatehouses have two portcullises (one at each end of the passage) creating a "killing zone."
- **Drawbridge (if moated):** A timber bridge that can be raised by chains attached to counterweights inside the gatehouse

### Interior

**The Passage:**
- A vaulted stone tunnel, 20-30 feet long, 10-15 feet wide, 12-15 feet high
- Stone-paved floor, worn by centuries of cart wheels and horse hooves
- **Murder holes:** Square openings in the ceiling of the passage through which defenders can drop:
  - Rocks, boiling water (not oil -- too expensive), quicklime, crossbow bolts
- **Arrow loops:** Slits in the side walls covering the passage
- The portcullis grooves are visible as vertical channels in the stone walls
- Gate recesses: shallow alcoves in the walls where the opened gates fold back

**Portcullis Mechanism Room (above the passage):**
- A windlass room with a large oak drum (the windlass), wound by two or more men using handles
- Iron chains run from the windlass through holes in the floor to the top of the portcullis
- A ratchet mechanism prevents the portcullis from dropping unless the pawl is released
- Counterweights (stone blocks on iron chains) assist in lifting
- A wooden frame supports the mechanism
- The room is open on one side to the wall-walk for access

**Guard Chambers (in the flanking towers):**
- Each tower has 2-3 floors of rooms:
  - **Ground floor:** A guard room with a table, stools, a brazier, weapon rack, and a locked chest for toll money. A barred window overlooks the approach. The gate keys hang on a hook.
  - **Upper floors:** Living quarters for the gate captain -- a bedchamber, a small sitting room with a fireplace. In royal gatehouses, these rooms can be quite lavish (the owner's primary residence).
  - **Roof level:** Open parapet with crenellations and arrow loops, offering a commanding view and firing positions

**Toll/Inspection Area (just inside the gate):**
- A covered area or lean-to shelter inside the walls where carts are stopped and inspected
- A table for the toll collector with scales, a measure, and a moneybox
- A barrier (wooden pole or chain) across the road that can be raised

### Textures and Colors
- Heavy grey stone throughout -- the most fortress-like building in the settlement
- Iron: massive portcullis bars, gate studs, chain links, locks -- all darkened with oil to prevent rust
- Oak: the gates are blackened with age and iron-stained from the hardware
- The passage interior is permanently in shadow

### Lighting
- The passage is dim even at midday -- natural light enters from both ends but the vault overhead traps shadow
- Guard rooms: small windows, candle lanterns, a fireplace
- Mechanism room: near darkness -- lit by a lantern when operating the portcullis
- The approach is deliberately shadowed to disorient attackers

### Atmosphere/Smell Cues
- Stone, iron, and cold: the passage funnels a perpetual draft
- Horse manure and cart-wheel grease on the passage floor
- Smoke from the guard room's brazier
- The creak and groan of the portcullis chains when the gate is opened or closed
- The echoing clip-clop of horses through the vaulted passage
- A sense of controlled power -- this is where authority is visible and tangible

## 4.5 Dungeon / Prison Cells

### Purpose
Confinement of prisoners awaiting trial, punishment, or ransom. Not primarily designed for long-term imprisonment (that is a later concept); medieval prisons hold people temporarily. Conditions range from tolerable (for wealthy prisoners who can pay for better treatment) to deliberately horrific (for those the lord wishes to break).

### Exterior Appearance
- Located within a castle keep, beneath a tower, or in the cellars of a town hall -- rarely a standalone building
- Access: a single iron-bound door in a castle corridor, or a trapdoor in the floor of the guard room
- The oubliette (a particularly cruel design): a bottle-shaped pit accessible only through a hole in the ceiling

### Interior

**The Approach/Corridor:**
- A narrow stone passage, barely 3 feet wide, descending by stone steps or a steep slope
- Dripping water from above, pooling in the uneven floor
- Iron wall sconces for torches (usually unlit -- a torch is carried by the jailer)
- Heavy iron-bound oak doors with small barred viewing grilles at each cell
- A massive iron bolt and a large iron key lock on each door

**Standard Cell:**
- **Size:** 6-8 feet square, 6-7 feet high. Barely room to lie down.
- **Walls:** Rough-hewn stone, damp and cold. Graffiti scratched into the stone by previous prisoners -- names, dates, prayers, crude drawings, tally marks counting days
- **Floor:** Stone, uneven, with a thin layer of rotting straw
- **Furniture:** None, or a stone bench against one wall (the prisoner's bed and seat)
- **Chains:** Iron ring bolts set into the walls at wrist and ankle height. Iron manacles connected by short chains. Some prisoners are chained standing, unable to sit or lie.
- **Sanitation:** A bucket (emptied irregularly) or a drain hole in the floor corner
- **Light:** A tiny window slit near the ceiling (if the cell is at or near ground level) admitting a thin blade of grey light. Underground cells: no light whatsoever except when the jailer opens the door.
- **Ventilation:** Minimal -- the air is stale and fetid
- **Water:** A drip from the wall that the prisoner cups in their hands, or a bowl of water pushed through the door slot once daily

**The Oubliette:**
- A vertical shaft 10-20 feet deep, 5-6 feet in diameter
- The prisoner is lowered on a rope or ladder through a circular hole in the floor above
- No light, no exit, no visitors
- A small drain at the bottom (sometimes choked)
- The name means "forgotten place" -- prisoners were left to die

**The Torture Chamber (if present):**
- A larger room adjacent to the cells
- Iron rings in the walls and ceiling for suspending prisoners
- A rack (a wooden frame with rollers at each end, connected by ropes to the prisoner's wrists and ankles)
- A brazier with heating irons (brands, pokers)
- A table with implements: thumbscrews, an iron boot (for crushing feet), pincers, knives
- A water bucket and ladle (for reviving unconscious prisoners)
- A wooden chair with arm and leg restraints
- A drain in the floor

### Textures and Colors
- **Stone:** Dark grey to black, permanently wet in places. White mineral deposits streak the walls. Green slime and mold near water seepage.
- **Iron:** Rust-red and rust-brown everywhere -- manacles, bolts, door hardware, all corroded from moisture. Some bright where recently used.
- **Straw:** Darkened and rotting, no longer golden -- more brown-black
- **Overall palette:** Black, dark grey, rust-red, sickly green -- no warmth or light

### Lighting
- Almost none. The defining characteristic is darkness.
- A torch brought by the jailer creates harsh, flickering orange light that makes the stone walls seem to move and the shadows leap
- The tiny window slit (if present) creates a single pale bar of light that shifts imperceptibly through the day -- the prisoner's only connection to the outside world

### Atmosphere/Smell Cues
- The foulest place in the settlement
- Human waste (the bucket, the floor, the straw)
- Mildew and rot
- Damp stone and stagnant water
- Unwashed bodies
- Fear-sweat: a particular acrid, metallic scent
- Rust (the constant presence of corroding iron)
- In the torture chamber: blood (metallic, coppery), burnt flesh, hot iron
- Sounds: dripping water (constant, maddening), distant footsteps, the rattle of chains, a prisoner's moan or prayer from a neighboring cell, rats scuttling
- Cold: the temperature is constant, year-round, and cold enough to be deeply uncomfortable
- Dark fantasy: an oppressive psychic weight -- suffering has soaked into the stones. Something watches from the darkest corner.

## 5.1 Town Hall / Guild Hall

### Purpose
The administrative and political center of the town. Where the town council meets, laws are proclaimed, disputes are arbitrated, taxes are collected, and civic events are hosted. Also houses the town archive and treasury. Guild halls serve a similar function for a specific trade guild -- meeting place, regulation center, and social club.

### Exterior Appearance
- **Structure:** The most impressive civic building -- stone or high-quality timber frame, 2-3 stories, with a prominent position on the market square
- **Ground floor:** Open arcaded undercroft serving as a covered market space -- stone columns supporting arches, with the market stalls sheltered beneath
- **Upper floors:** The council chamber and offices -- an imposing facade with large mullioned windows, often with an oriel (projecting bay window) from the council chamber
- **Tower:** Some town halls have a clock tower or bell tower with a bell for summoning the council and marking hours
- **Heraldry:** The town coat of arms carved in stone above the entrance or painted on the facade
- **Entrance:** A wide stone staircase leading from the market square to the upper-floor entrance (the ground floor is market, the upper floor is government)

### Interior Rooms

**Market Undercroft (Ground Floor):**
- A large open space with stone columns and vaulted ceiling
- During market days: tables and stalls fill the space
- On non-market days: relatively empty, echoing stone
- Standardized weights and measures displayed on a table (the town's official measures, against which all merchants must calibrate)
- A set of town stocks or pillory visible in the market square outside

**Council Chamber (Upper Floor):**
- The main room: a large rectangular space with a high timber-beamed ceiling
- A heavy oak council table (or horseshoe-shaped table) with carved high-backed chairs for the aldermen
- The mayor's chair (the largest, with arms and possibly a canopy) at the head
- A lectern for reading proclamations
- A writing desk for the town clerk with inkwells, quills, sand-shaker, wax seals, and parchment rolls
- A large iron-bound chest for the town treasury (double-locked, requiring two keys held by different officials)
- The town charter, framed and displayed on the wall
- Tapestries or painted cloth hangings depicting the town's history or patron saint
- A fireplace with the town arms carved on the overmantel
- Candle chandeliers (wrought iron or brass)
- A cupboard with official pewter and silver plate for civic banquets
- The town seal and stamp

**Deliberation Room:**
- A smaller private room off the council chamber, enclosed by wooden paneled walls with a lockable door
- Used for private discussion by the aldermen during public trials
- A table, chairs, a candle, and a jug of wine

**Archive/Treasury Room:**
- A locked room with iron-bound chests and shelving
- Rolls of parchment (deeds, charters, tax records, court records) in labeled wooden pigeonholes
- Account books (leather-bound, heavy)
- The town's standard weights, measures, and seals
- Bags of coin in locked strongboxes

**Court Room (if combined with tribunal):**
- See Court/Tribunal (5.2)

### Textures and Colors
- **Civic grandeur:** Richer than residential buildings -- carved stone details, painted plaster, gilded woodwork
- **Stone:** Dressed and clean-cut, lighter in color (often whitewashed or pale limestone)
- **Timber:** High-quality oak, carved and polished -- dark brown with warm highlights
- **Heraldry:** Bright colors on shields, banners, and painted panels -- the town's colors prominently displayed
- **Documents:** Cream-brown parchment with dark ink and red wax seals

### Lighting
- Well-lit: large windows (representing transparency and openness of governance)
- Candle chandeliers for evening meetings
- The council chamber is the brightest upper room on the square

### Atmosphere/Smell Cues
- Beeswax polish on the furniture and floor
- Parchment and ink
- Wood smoke from the fireplace
- A lingering hint of wine and food from civic banquets
- The gravitas of authority -- formal, slightly intimidating
- In guild halls: the specific smell of the trade (leather for the tanners' guild, wool for the weavers') from members' clothing

## 5.2 Court / Tribunal

### Purpose
A public space where justice is administered -- trials, sentencing, and sometimes execution of punishments. May be a dedicated room in the town hall or a separate building. The lord or magistrate presides, with a jury of townsmen in some cases.

### Exterior Appearance
- Often part of the town hall (see above) rather than a standalone building
- If separate: a stone building with a prominent entrance, possibly with carved allegorical figures (Justice, Mercy) flanking the door

### Interior

**Courtroom:**
- A high-ceilinged room designed to impress and intimidate
- **The Bench:** A raised platform (dais) at one end where the judge/magistrate sits behind a heavy oak desk. The seat is high-backed, ornate, under a carved wooden canopy or beneath the coat of arms of the presiding authority.
- **The Dock:** A wooden enclosure (waist-high railing) where the accused stands, facing the bench. An iron ring in the floor or railing for chaining violent prisoners.
- **Public gallery:** Wooden benches or standing room for spectators -- the court is public
- **Jury box (if applicable):** A partitioned area with seating for 12 jurymen
- **Clerk's table:** Below the bench, a desk for the court clerk with quill, ink, parchment, and the case records
- **Witness stand:** A small raised platform beside the bench
- **The Bible/Holy Book:** On a stand for swearing oaths
- **A set of manacles:** On the dock railing
- **Punishment implements (visible as deterrent):** A pillory collar, a branding iron on a bracket, a whipping post in the courtyard outside -- visible through a window
- **Floor:** Stone or tile, worn smooth

### Textures and Colors
- Dark oak paneling on the walls (sombre, authoritative)
- The bench is polished and imposing
- Iron in the dock fixtures
- Parchment and wax seals on the clerk's table
- Overall: deliberately dark and formal -- the room is designed to inspire fear and respect

### Lighting
- Moderate: tall windows provide natural light but the dark wood absorbs it, keeping the atmosphere serious
- Candles on the bench and clerk's desk
- The accused in the dock is lit by a window or candle arrangement that makes them visible to the court

### Atmosphere/Smell Cues
- Tension and fear
- Beeswax polish and old wood
- Sweat from the crowd and the accused
- Ink and parchment
- The echoing voice of the magistrate in the stone room
- The jangle of chains when the accused is brought in

## 5.3 Library / Archive

### Purpose
A repository of books, manuscripts, and records. In the medieval period, libraries are attached to monasteries, cathedrals, or (later) universities and wealthy households. A standalone civic library is rare but exists in some wealthy Italian and Flemish cities. The books are the most valuable objects in the settlement -- each one representing months or years of a scribe's labor.

### Exterior Appearance
- Typically an upper-floor room in a larger building (monastery, cathedral, university) -- the upper floor protects against damp
- If a dedicated building: stone walls, large windows (facing north for consistent light), a heavy lockable door

### Interior

**The Reading Room:**
- **Layout:** A long, relatively narrow room with windows on one or both long walls
- **Lecterns:** In early libraries (pre-1400): long sloping desks (lecterns) run perpendicular to the windows, between each pair of windows. Books lie on the lecterns, chained by iron chains attached to a rod running along the lectern. The reader stands or sits on a bench and reads the book in place -- it cannot be removed.
- **Shelving (later medieval):** As book collections grew, shelving was added beneath and behind the lecterns. Books stored spine-hidden (an identifying mark on the page edges faces outward), lying flat.
- **Chained books:** Each book is attached to its lectern or shelf by an iron chain (12-24 inches long), connected to a metal clasp riveted to one cover of the book. The chains are long enough to allow the book to be opened and read but not removed.
- **Reading desks:** Individual carrels (small enclosed desks) along the windows for study, each with a sloped reading surface, an inkwell, and a quill for note-taking. A candle holder (used sparingly -- fire risk).
- **Catalogue:** A leather-bound catalogue listing every volume, its location, and a brief description
- **Ladder:** A small wooden ladder for reaching upper shelves

**The Archive Room (if separate):**
- A locked, dry room with wooden pigeonholes and shelving
- Rolled parchment documents in labeled compartments
- Bound registers (tax rolls, court records, deeds, charters)
- Wax seal impressions and matrices stored in boxes
- A work table for the archivist

### Textures and Colors
- **Dominant:** Warm brown leather (book covers), cream parchment, dark oak (lecterns and shelving), the glint of iron chains
- **Books:** Leather bindings in brown, red, and occasionally blue or green. Some with brass corner protectors and clasps. The largest books are folio-sized (15x20 inches).
- **Parchment:** Warm cream to pale yellow, with dark brown/black ink and occasional red rubrics and blue/gold illuminated capitals
- **Wood:** Dark polished oak lecterns and shelving, worn smooth
- **Metal:** Iron chains (dark grey, slightly rusty at the joints), brass clasps (warm gold patina)

### Lighting
- **Natural light:** Essential -- large windows let in as much daylight as possible. The room is oriented for maximum consistent light.
- **Candles:** Used very sparingly due to the extreme fire risk. Only a single candle at a reading desk, and never left unattended.
- **Ambience:** Bright and quiet during the day; the library closes at sunset

### Atmosphere/Smell Cues
- **Old books:** A distinctive, beloved smell -- the slightly sweet, vanilla-like scent of aging parchment and leather combined with the faint organic smell of the hide (vellum) from which the pages are made
- **Dust:** fine parchment dust and the mineral smell of old stone
- **Ink:** a faint iron-gall tang (the ink used medieval manuscripts has an acrid, metallic component)
- **Silence:** Enforced and profound. The scratch of a quill, the rattle of a chain when a book is turned, a distant cough
- **The weight of knowledge:** Row upon row of laboriously copied texts, some centuries old, each representing enormous human effort
- **Dark fantasy:** Some books are locked away in a separate cupboard -- banned texts, grimoires, heretical works. The librarian has the only key. The chains rattle softly when no one is moving.

## 5.4 Bathhouse

### Purpose
A public facility for bathing, socializing, and (in some cases) illicit activities. Medieval bathhouses existed throughout Europe, more common than popular imagination suggests. They serve hygiene, medical (curative baths), and social functions. Hot water baths, steam rooms, and sometimes hair-cutting and minor medical procedures (cupping, bloodletting) are offered.

### Exterior Appearance
- **Structure:** A stone building, often with a tall chimney from the furnace. Located near a water source (river, well, or spring). One story with a high-ceilinged bathing area.
- **Sign:** A painted sign showing bathing implements (comb, scissors, basin) or a striped pole (the barber-surgeon's symbol, as bathhouses often employed them).
- **Entrance:** Separate entrances for men and women (or designated days for each).

### Interior Rooms

**Undressing Room (Apodyterium):**
- Wooden benches along the walls
- Wooden pegs and hooks for hanging clothing
- Shelving or cubby holes with numbered slots for valuables (no locks -- an attendant watches)
- Linen towels available (a luxury)
- A wooden duckboard floor (slatted boards over the stone) to keep feet dry

**Hot Room / Steam Room (Caldarium):**
- The heart of the bathhouse
- **Heating:** A hypocaust system (borrowed from Roman design) heats the floor and walls: a furnace in an adjacent room sends hot air through channels beneath a raised stone or brick floor (supported on short brick pillars -- pilae). The floor is warm to hot; wooden sandals (clogs) are provided.
- **Tubs:** Large wooden tubs (big enough for 2-4 people) filled with heated water, sometimes with added herbs (lavender, rosemary, or medicinal herbs). Tubs lined with linen to prevent splinters.
- **Steam:** Water splashed on heated stones produces steam. Bathers sit on stone or wooden benches and sweat.
- **Scrubbing:** Attendants scrub bathers with rough linen cloths or birch branches
- **Accessories:** Pumice stones for calluses, soap (soft soap made from tallow and wood ash), combs, scissors, razors (for haircuts and shaving), cupping glasses (for medical bloodletting)
- **Floor:** Warm stone (heated from below), with a drainage channel to carry used water to the outside

**Warm Room (Tepidarium):**
- A transitional room between the hot and cold areas
- Heated moderately
- Benches for resting
- Where conversations happen and socializing occurs
- Food and drink may be served here: ale, wine, bread, cheese

**Cold Plunge (Frigidarium):**
- A stone or wooden plunge pool filled with cold water
- For invigorating dips after the hot room
- Stone steps descending into the pool
- Smaller than the hot room

**Furnace Room (Praefurnium):**
- Behind the hot room, inaccessible to bathers
- A large brick furnace fed with wood
- A copper or iron boiler for heating water
- The furnace keeper (stoker) tends the fire -- hot, dirty, and exhausting work
- Ducts and channels directing hot air beneath the floor

### Textures and Colors
- **Stone:** Warm-toned (from the heat), with white mineral deposits from steam
- **Brick:** Red-brown, especially around the furnace
- **Wood:** The tubs are pale golden oak or elm, darkened by constant moisture. The benches are smooth from wet use.
- **Water:** Steam-foggy, with a slight cloudy quality from soap and herbs
- **Linen:** White towels and tub linings, slightly grey from use
- **Tile:** Where used, decorative tile in warm reds, blues, and greens (especially in wealthier establishments)

### Lighting
- **Hot room:** Dim and steamy -- small high windows let in diffuse light. The steam creates a soft, hazy atmosphere where figures are half-visible.
- **Warm room:** Better lit with larger windows
- **Furnace room:** The orange glow of the fire

### Atmosphere/Smell Cues
- Steam and warm water: the dominant sensory experience
- Lavender, rosemary, or other bath herbs
- Soft soap: a slightly alkaline, fatty scent
- Wood smoke from the furnace (leaking slightly into the bathing areas)
- Wet stone and wet wood
- Human bodies (sweat, skin, hair)
- The warmth is enveloping -- a stark contrast to the cold medieval world outside
- Sounds: splashing water, murmured conversation, the creak of wooden tubs, distant stoking of the furnace

## 6.1 Farmhouse with Attached Barn

### Purpose
Combined dwelling and agricultural workspace for a tenant farmer or free peasant. Slightly larger and more prosperous than a peasant cottage. The farmhouse shelters the family; the barn stores grain, hay, and houses livestock. In many regions these are a single building (a longhouse or byre-dwelling) with the family at one end and animals at the other.

### Exterior Appearance
- **Farmhouse section:** Similar to the peasant cottage but larger (25-35 feet long). Wattle-and-daub or stone walls, thatch or slate roof. May have a stone chimney (a sign of relative prosperity). Two rooms instead of one.
- **Barn section:** Attached or very close. Taller than the farmhouse (to accommodate hay storage in the loft). Timber-framed with vertical boarding (oak or elm planks), a large pair of barn doors (wide enough for a loaded hay cart), and a smaller door on the opposite side (creating a cross-draft for threshing).
- **Yard:** A muddy, trampled yard between house and barn with a well, a woodpile, a midden heap, a chicken run (wicker fence enclosure), a vegetting garden, and a pigsty.
- **Nearby:** Open field strips (the farmer's allocated land in the common field system), a meadow, possibly an orchard of a few fruit trees

### Interior Rooms

**Farmhouse -- Hall/Kitchen:**
- Similar to a larger peasant cottage but with a dedicated hearth (possibly a simple chimney rather than open smoke hole)
- A trestle table and benches for the family (6-10 people)
- A wider range of cooking equipment: an iron pot, a skillet, a spit, a bread paddle, a hand-mill (quern)
- A meal chest (for storing milled flour)
- A bacon rack above the fire -- sides of bacon or a ham hanging in the smoke to cure
- A butter churn in the corner (a tall wooden cylinder with a plunger)
- A cheese press
- Strings of dried apple slices, onions, and garlic hanging from the beams

**Farmhouse -- Sleeping Room (if divided):**
- A low partition (wattle or plank) separates the sleeping area from the hall
- The farmer and wife have a simple bed frame; children sleep on palliasses
- A wooden chest for clothing and valuables
- A cradle for the infant

**Barn -- Threshing Floor:**
- The central ground-level area between the two large doors
- A hardened clay or plank floor where sheaves of grain are spread and beaten with flails (two sticks connected by a leather thong) to separate the grain from the stalks
- Winnowing: the grain is tossed on flat baskets in the cross-draft, allowing the lighter chaff to blow away

**Barn -- Animal Byre:**
- One end of the barn: stalls for 2-4 cows, divided by wooden posts and rails
- A hay rack (manger) at each stall
- A trough for water
- Straw bedding on the earth floor
- Milking stools and wooden buckets
- A cat (for mice control)

**Barn -- Hay Loft:**
- Above the animal byre or threshing floor
- Accessed by a wooden ladder
- Loose hay or straw piled deep
- Also stores tools: scythes, sickles, wooden rakes, pitchforks (wooden or iron-tipped), hoes, a plow blade (stored for the off-season)

### Textures and Colors
- **Earth and wood:** Brown, golden-straw, grey-green, warm amber
- The barn interior is dominated by the golden color and sweet smell of hay
- Everything is slightly dirty -- farming is muddy work
- The yard is a mix of mud, straw, and animal droppings

### Lighting
- Farmhouse: hearth fire, window shutters open during the day
- Barn: daylight through gaps in the vertical boards (creating dramatic stripes of light), the barn doors wide open during work

### Atmosphere/Smell Cues
- The farmhouse smells of cooking, wood smoke, and animals (they are very nearby)
- The barn: sweet hay, warm cow breath, manure, grain dust, leather tack
- The yard: fresh earth after rain, animal dung, wood smoke, the green scent of the kitchen garden
- Sound: lowing cattle, clucking chickens, the bark of a farm dog, the creak of the well

## 6.2 Windmill / Watermill

### Purpose
Grinds grain into flour using wind or water power. The miller is a key (and often resented) figure -- every farmer must bring their grain here for grinding, paying a toll (a portion of flour). The mill is one of the most technologically sophisticated buildings in the settlement.

### Exterior Appearance -- Windmill
- **Structure:** A timber-framed tower (round or polygonal), 30-40 feet tall, mounted on a central post (post mill) that allows the entire building to rotate to face the wind, or a fixed stone tower (tower mill) with a rotating wooden cap that carries the sails.
- **Sails:** Four large canvas-covered wooden lattice sails (each 20-30 feet long), mounted on a windshaft. The canvas is reefed (rolled up) in high winds.
- **Cap:** The top section (either the whole building or just the cap) pivots on a greased post. A tail pole extends to the ground for manual rotation.

### Exterior Appearance -- Watermill
- **Structure:** A stone or timber building straddling a millrace (an artificial channel diverting water from a river or stream). One wall has a large opening where the water wheel is visible.
- **Water wheel:** A large wooden wheel (10-20 feet in diameter) with paddles or buckets. Types: undershot (turned by water passing beneath), overshot (water pours over the top -- more efficient), or breastshot.
- **Millpond:** An artificial pond upstream, controlled by a sluice gate, providing a consistent head of water.

### Interior

**Grinding Floor:**
- The heart of the mill: two large circular millstones (4-5 feet in diameter, 6-12 inches thick), the lower one (bedstone) fixed, the upper one (runner stone) rotating.
- The runner stone is turned by the gear mechanism connected to the water wheel or windshaft
- A wooden hopper (a square funnel) mounted above the stones feeds grain between them
- A spout below the stones channels flour into a wooden trough or sack
- The stones are enclosed in a wooden casing (the tun or vat) to contain the flour
- A mechanism to raise/lower the runner stone controls the fineness of the grind
- Flour dust coats every surface -- an explosion hazard (the miller knows to avoid naked flames)

**Gear Mechanism:**
- Large wooden toothed gears (pit wheel, wallower, great spur wheel) transfer power from the water wheel or windshaft to the millstones
- The gears are made of oak or elm, with hardwood teeth (cogs) inserted into the rim -- replaceable when worn
- A clutch mechanism allows the stones to be disengaged
- The machinery creaks, groans, and rumbles when operating -- a constant, vibrating rhythm

**Storage Areas:**
- Sacks of unground grain stacked on one side
- Sacks of finished flour on the other
- A scale and weights for measuring the miller's toll
- Shelving for spare parts: extra cogs, leather belting, tool kit (chisels, hammers, a mill bill for re-dressing the stone grooves)

**Miller's Living Quarters (if integrated):**
- A small living area in the mill or an attached cottage
- Simple furnishings like a farmhouse
- Everything coated in flour dust

### Textures and Colors
- **Flour:** White-cream dust on every surface, in the air, on the miller's clothing and hair
- **Millstones:** Pale grey (Derbyshire grit or French burr stone), with radiating furrows (called "lands and furrows") cut into the grinding face
- **Wood:** The gears and structure are massive oak and elm, darkened with age and greased with tallow
- **Water (watermill):** The constant sound and sight of flowing water, the wet wood of the wheel, green algae on the millrace walls

### Lighting
- Small windows (flour dust + open flame = disaster), so relatively dim
- The grinding floor is lit by daylight from the grain hopper opening and small windows
- The waterwheel chamber is damp and dark

### Atmosphere/Smell Cues
- **Flour dust:** Hangs in the air, tickles the throat, visible in light beams
- **Ground grain:** A warm, nutty, slightly sweet smell -- fresh flour is more aromatic than store-bought
- **Damp wood and water** (watermill): a mossy, organic smell
- **Grease and oil:** from the wooden gear mechanisms
- **Vibration:** The entire building vibrates and rumbles when operating -- the stones create a deep, rhythmic hum felt in the bones
- **Sound:** The rush of water (watermill), the creak and clatter of gears, the rumble of stones, the flap of canvas sails (windmill)

## 6.3 Granary / Storehouse

### Purpose
Stores threshed grain (wheat, barley, oats, rye) and other dry goods safe from moisture, vermin, and theft. A critical building -- the grain stored here feeds the settlement through winter and provides seed for next year's planting.

### Exterior Appearance
- **Structure:** A small stone or timber building, raised off the ground on stone mushroom-shaped staddle stones (flat caps prevent rats from climbing up) or on wooden posts. The raised floor is the key design feature.
- **Size:** 10-20 feet square, single story
- **Walls:** Timber boarding (tightly fitted to prevent vermin) or stone
- **Door:** A single heavy door with a large lock (grain theft is a capital crime in some periods)
- **Ventilation:** Small louvered openings near the roof for air circulation to prevent mold

### Interior
- **Floor:** Raised timber planking, swept clean
- **Storage:** Grain stored in large wooden bins or in sacks stacked on pallets (keeping them off the floor for air circulation)
- **Measuring equipment:** A bushel measure (a standard-sized wooden bucket), a scale
- **Tools:** A wooden grain shovel, a rake for leveling grain in bins
- **Pest control:** A cat (essential), traps, and possibly herbs (mint, bay leaves) believed to deter vermin
- **Inspection:** The grain must be periodically turned and checked for mold, weevils, or dampness

### Textures and Colors
- Pale golden grain, dark brown wood, grey stone staddle stones
- Dry and clean -- the driest building in the settlement

### Lighting
- Dim -- small windows/louvers only. A lantern is carried in for inspection (carefully -- grain dust is flammable)

### Atmosphere/Smell Cues
- Dry grain: a warm, nutty, slightly dusty scent
- Old wood
- Very quiet -- just the soft rustle of grain settling
- The granary is a place of security and anxiety in equal measure -- a full granary means survival; an empty one means starvation

---

## 6.4 Stable / Horse Barn

### Purpose
Houses horses (riding horses, warhorses, draft horses) and their tack and feed. May also stable mules and donkeys. A well-run stable is essential for military readiness and trade.

### Exterior Appearance
- **Structure:** A long, low timber or stone building, single story with a hayloft above. Wide doors at one or both ends.
- **Size:** 40-80 feet long, 15-20 feet wide, depending on the number of horses
- **Yard:** A paddock or exercise yard adjacent, enclosed by a wooden rail fence

### Interior

**Stalls:**
- Two rows of stalls facing a central aisle (6-8 feet wide), divided by wooden partitions (4-5 feet high)
- Each stall: 10-12 feet deep, 5-6 feet wide
- Stall furniture:
  - A wooden hay rack (manger) at the front, chest height
  - A water bucket (wooden or leather) hanging from a hook
  - A tie ring (iron) bolted to the wall for securing the horse's halter rope
  - Straw bedding on the earth floor, 6-12 inches deep
  - A salt lick (a block of rock salt) in a wooden holder

**Tack Room:**
- A separate small room (lockable) at one end
- Wall-mounted wooden saddle racks (saddles stored upside-down to keep their shape)
- Bridles, halters, and lead ropes on hooks
- Spare horseshoes and nails in a box
- Grooming tools: curry combs (metal), body brushes (stiff bristle), hoof picks, mane combs
- Leather cleaning supplies: saddle soap, neat's-foot oil, cloths
- Blankets (horse rugs) folded on shelves

**Feed Storage:**
- Bins of oats and barley
- A hay store (may be in the loft or an adjacent stack)
- Bran mash ingredients
- A feed measure

**Hayloft:**
- Above the stalls, accessed by a ladder or wooden stairs
- Loose hay piled deep, pushed down through openings (hay doors) in the loft floor to the mangers below
- A large exterior hay door in the gable end for loading from a hay wagon

### Textures and Colors
- Golden hay and straw, warm brown wood, dark leather tack, grey iron hardware
- The stalls are darkened with age and horse oils

### Lighting
- Natural light through the wide doors, high windows, and gaps in the boarding
- The hayloft is dim, with beams of light through cracks -- very atmospheric
- No open flames (fire risk with hay)

### Atmosphere/Smell Cues
- **The warm, complex smell of horses:** A mix of hay, sweat, leather, manure, and the particular sweet, musky scent of horse itself
- Hay: sweet and dry
- Leather and neat's-foot oil from the tack room
- Manure and wet straw (not as unpleasant as it sounds -- a warm, earthy smell that horse people find comforting)
- Sound: the stamp and blow of horses in their stalls, the clink of halter chains, the soft munching of hay, the occasional whinny, sparrows in the rafters

## 6.5 Chicken Coop / Pig Pen

### Purpose
Small-scale animal housing for poultry and pigs -- the most common livestock kept by all but the poorest households. Chickens provide eggs and occasional meat; pigs eat scraps and provide pork, bacon, lard, and sausage.

### Exterior Appearance -- Chicken Coop
- **Structure:** A small timber structure (4-6 feet square, 4-5 feet high) with a sloped roof of thatch or boards. Raised slightly off the ground on posts.
- **Access:** A small hinged door for the chickens (closed at night against foxes), and a larger access panel for the keeper to collect eggs
- **Run:** A wattle-fenced enclosure (the chicken run) attached, allowing the birds to scratch in the dirt

### Interior -- Chicken Coop
- Roosting poles: horizontal sticks (broomstick thickness) at various heights where chickens perch at night
- Nesting boxes: 3-6 small compartments (12 inches square) lined with straw, where hens lay eggs
- Floor: straw bedding over earth, thick with droppings
- A water dish (shallow ceramic) and a feed trough (wooden)

### Exterior Appearance -- Pig Pen (Sty)
- **Structure:** A low stone or timber-walled enclosure (3-4 feet high walls) with a small roofed shelter at one end
- **Mud:** The pen floor is churned to deep mud by the pigs' rooting
- **Trough:** A heavy stone or wooden feeding trough, untiggable

### Interior -- Pig Shelter
- A simple lean-to or small hut within the pen
- Straw bedding
- Room for 1-4 pigs to lie together for warmth
- A stone or wooden feeding trough

### Textures and Colors
- Chicken coop: straw yellow, white feathers, speckled brown eggs, wood brown
- Pig pen: mud brown (every shade), grey stone, straw gold
- Both are perpetually dirty and organic

### Lighting
- Open to the sky (pen) or with small ventilation gaps (coop). Natural light.

### Atmosphere/Smell Cues
- **Chicken coop:** Sharp, acrid ammonia from droppings; dusty feathers; the warm, dry smell of straw; clucking and scratching sounds
- **Pig pen:** Rich, heavy, earthy smell of pig manure and churned mud; the grunting and squealing of pigs; wet mud squelching underfoot
- Both are "honest farm smells" -- unpleasant to visitors but routine to farmers

## 7.1 Witch's Hut

### Purpose
Home and workshop of a hedge witch, wise woman, or practitioner of folk magic. Exists on the boundary between civilization and the wild -- physically located at the forest's edge, a swamp margin, or an isolated clearing. The witch provides healing, divination, curses, love potions, and midwifery to villagers who come secretly despite the Church's disapproval.

### Exterior Appearance
- **Structure:** An old, organic-looking cottage that seems to have grown rather than been built. Timber frame, but the posts are barely squared -- some still have bark. Wattle-and-daub walls, but the daub is mixed with moss and the wattle shows through in places. The building leans slightly, as if the ground itself has shifted.
- **Roof:** Thick thatch, heavily overgrown with moss, lichen, and even small plants growing from the accumulated debris. A crooked chimney of piled stones, trailing thin blue-grey smoke.
- **Garden:** Instead of a neat kitchen garden, the surroundings are a wild tangle of deliberately cultivated medicinal and magical plants: wolfsbane (monkshood), belladonna (deadly nightshade), mandrake, hemlock, henbane, wormwood, and mugwort, alongside common herbs like sage, yarrow, and elderberry. A gnarled elder tree stands near the door.
- **Fence:** A crooked fence of twisted branches, with small bone charms, feather bundles, and knotted cord talismans tied to the posts (protective wards).
- **Door:** A low door of thick planks, darkened with age. A horseshoe nailed above it (iron against the fae). A bundle of rowan berries tied to the latch. Scratches around the threshold that might be runes.
- **Other:** Drying racks with herbs and mushrooms outside. A cauldron on a stone hearth in the yard. A chopping stump with a hand-axe. A well with a bucket, the water said to have healing properties.

### Interior -- Single Room

**The Hearth:**
- A crude stone fireplace with a cast-iron cauldron suspended over a low fire of apple, birch, and hazel wood (each wood chosen for its magical properties)
- The cauldron contains a simmering preparation -- greenish, fragrant, bubbling slowly
- Bundles of herbs drying on a rack above the fire, slowly smoking in the heat

**The Ceiling:**
- Covered in hanging things: bundles of dried herbs (every conceivable plant), strings of mushrooms, dried flowers, garlic braids, animal parts (a rabbit's foot, a dried toad, crow feathers, a snakeskin), knotted cords, bags of unknown contents, and small bones on strings that click and rattle in the draft from the fire

**Shelves and Storage:**
- Rough wooden shelves on every wall, crammed with:
  - Glass and ceramic jars of every size containing: dried herbs, powdered minerals, preserved specimens in murky liquid (a pickled newt, floating eyes, roots that look like fingers)
  - Small cloth bags tied with colored thread (each color signifying a different purpose)
  - Crystals and stones: a chunk of quartz, a smooth river stone with a natural hole (a hag stone), an amethyst cluster, a piece of lodite (lodestone/magnetite)
  - Candle stubs in every color (black, red, white, green) -- each used for different rituals
  - A human skull on the top shelf, decorated with painted symbols

**The Work Table:**
- A heavy, stained oak table in the center of the room. On it:
  - A mortar and pestle (stone, well-used, stained dark)
  - A cutting board with a herb knife (the blade has a slightly curved, crescent-moon shape)
  - A set of small weighing scales (for measuring ingredients precisely)
  - Scattered petals, leaf fragments, and powder
  - A leather-bound book of handwritten recipes, spells, and observations (the grimoire), its pages stiff with age, some marked with pressed flowers or smears of unidentifiable substances
  - Ink made from oak gall and iron, a crow-quill pen
  - A scrying bowl (a shallow black ceramic or obsidian bowl) filled with still water

**Divination Corner:**
- A small table or shelf dedicated to fortune-telling:
  - A set of carved bone rune stones in a leather pouch
  - A pack of hand-painted tarot cards (the images are unsettling)
  - A polished obsidian mirror (a scrying glass)
  - A pendulum (a crystal on a chain)
  - Dried yarrow stalks (for I Ching-style divination)

**Sleeping Area:**
- A narrow bed built into an alcove, more of a shelf with a straw mattress
- A patchwork quilt of many fabrics, some very old
- A cat (or raven, or toad) sleeping on the bed -- the witch's familiar
- Under the bed: a locked wooden box containing the most dangerous or valuable items

### Textures and Colors
- **Organic chaos:** Every surface is covered. The color palette is nature's own -- dried greens, brown bark, golden straw, the black of iron, the amber of old glass, the white of bone, the purple of dried lavender.
- **Dark spots:** Some areas are deliberately unsettling -- a jar of dark red liquid, a hand of glory (preserved hand), bones arranged in patterns
- **Patina:** Everything is old, worn, stained, and layered with the residue of decades of use

### Lighting
- The fire provides a warm, unsteady glow that makes the hanging objects cast moving shadows
- A few tallow candles in makeshift holders (iron nails driven into shelves, pools of wax on plates)
- Daylight through the small windows is filtered green by the overgrown garden outside
- The overall effect is dim, warm, and shifting -- things at the edge of vision seem to move

### Atmosphere/Smell Cues
- **Overwhelming herbal complexity:** Dozens of dried plants create a thick, layered scent -- lavender, sage, mugwort, wormwood, mint, and many unidentifiable botanical notes
- The sharp, acrid smoke of specific burning herbs (sage smudging, juniper)
- The animal musk of the familiar
- Something slightly rotten (a preservation jar that needs resealing, or an ingredient past its prime)
- Beeswax and tallow candles
- The damp, earthy smell of the forest pressing close outside
- A faint metallic or ozone tinge -- the smell of magic (or madness)
- Sound: the bubbling cauldron, the crackle of the fire, the click of bone charms in the draft, the familiar's breathing, birdsong from outside that sounds slightly wrong

## 7.2 Necromancer's Tower

### Purpose
The residence and laboratory of a necromancer -- a practitioner of death magic who communes with spirits, raises the dead, and studies the boundary between life and death. Located far from settlement, in a place of power: an ancient burial ground, a blighted hilltop, or the ruins of a desecrated church. The tower is both a home and a conduit for dark energies.

### Exterior Appearance
- **Structure:** A tall, narrow stone tower (40-60 feet high, 15-20 feet in diameter), either ancient and repurposed (a ruined watchtower or lighthouse) or built by the necromancer through a combination of mundane labor and magical compulsion (the stones themselves seem wrong -- mismatched, as if dragged from ruins).
- **Stone:** Black or dark grey stone, possibly volcanic basalt or slate. The mortar between the stones is darker than natural -- mixed with bone ash or blood. Thin veins of a sickly green-black substance trace the mortar joints like diseased capillaries.
- **Condition:** Parts of the tower look ancient and parts look impossibly new. Stone stairs spiral up the exterior for a few turns before entering the wall. Some stones are carved with symbols that hurt to look at.
- **Windows:** Narrow, irregularly placed, some glowing faintly from within at night -- not candlelight but a cold, pale luminescence. One window near the top is larger, facing north (toward the land of the dead in some traditions).
- **Roof:** A flat or slightly conical stone cap, sometimes with an iron weather vane in the shape of a raven, a skull, or a pointing skeletal hand.
- **Surroundings:** The ground around the tower is blighted -- grass is brown and sparse, trees are leafless and twisted, no birds sing nearby. A ring of standing stones or bone markers encircles the base. A low stone wall encloses a garden of poisonous plants and grave-mushrooms (pale, phosphorescent, growing in rings).
- **Smell from outside:** A sweet, cloying scent of decay mixed with chemical sharpness, detectable from a distance downwind.

### Interior

**Ground Floor -- Entry/Guard Room:**
- A heavy iron door with locks and magical wards (inscribed symbols that glow faintly when touched)
- A bare stone room serving as a buffer zone
- A summoned guardian: an animated skeleton standing motionless in a wall niche, or a bound spirit visible as a shimmer in the air
- Iron torch brackets (the torches burn with a pale, heatless flame -- magical)
- A spiral stone staircase ascending through the ceiling
- The floor has a carved circle of protection (a pentagram or other sigil) for containing summoned entities

**Second Floor -- Library/Study:**
- Bookshelves lining the curved walls, crammed with:
  - Forbidden grimoires bound in black leather (some in human skin)
  - Scrolls of necromantic formulae
  - Anatomical texts with disturbingly detailed illustrations
  - Historical chronicles and genealogies (the necromancer needs to know who is buried where)
  - A few mundane books -- philosophy, astronomy, medicine
- A writing desk with:
  - Ink (regular and a darker ink that seems to writhe when you look away)
  - Quills (crow feather and bone)
  - A skull used as a paperweight (it whispers sometimes)
  - Unfinished correspondence
  - Diagrams of ritual circles and anatomical charts
- A reading chair with a fur throw (the fur is from no natural animal -- too dark, too coarse)
- A fireplace (the flames burn blue or purple, a side effect of the tower's ambient energies)

**Third Floor -- The Laboratory:**
- The working space for necromantic experiments and rituals
- **Central table:** A massive stone slab (originally an altar, or possibly a mortuary slab) bearing:
  - A partially assembled skeleton, wired together with silver thread
  - Jars of preserving fluid containing organs, tissues, and less identifiable specimens
  - Alchemical apparatus: retorts, alembics, and distillation flasks, some containing luminous green or black liquids
  - Surgical tools: bone saws, scalpels, forceps, rib spreaders -- all immaculately clean
- **Shelving:**
  - Rows of jars with preserved body parts and specimens
  - Crystal phials of distilled essences (labeled in an occult language)
  - Powdered bone, graveyard dirt, mandrake root, nightshade extract
  - A human hand preserved in oil (a Hand of Glory -- a powerful dark talisman)
- **Ritual space:** A large area of the floor is a permanent ritual circle:
  - Inlaid with silver wire in complex geometric patterns (a summoning/binding circle)
  - Candelabra at five points, holding black candles
  - Scorch marks and dark stains within the circle from previous rituals
  - The air above the circle feels different -- thicker, colder, with a faint vibration
- **Cage/Cell:** A small iron cage in one corner for holding living subjects or containing uncontrolled undead

**Top Floor -- The Observatory/Sanctum:**
- The necromancer's private space, with the largest window
- An astronomical/astrological instrument (an armillary sphere or astrolabe) -- death magic is tied to celestial cycles
- A crystal ball or dark mirror for scrying beyond the veil
- A meditation circle with cushions
- A simple bed (the necromancer still needs sleep -- or tries to)
- A personal altar with offerings to the dead: a skull wreathed in dead flowers, a bowl of blood (dried), and a candle that never quite goes out

### Textures and Colors
- **Dominant palette:** Black, dark grey, sickly green, pale bone-white, silver, and the occasional jarring purple-black of magical residue
- **Stone:** Dark and cold, with an almost oily sheen in places where magical energy has saturated the material
- **Metal:** Tarnished silver (the ritual circles), black iron (doors, cages, tools), verdigris-green copper (alchemical apparatus)
- **Organic:** The yellowed bone of specimens, the brown-grey of preserved tissues, the papery dryness of ancient scrolls
- **Light effects:** Faint bioluminescence from certain jars and fungal growths; the eerie glow of magical flames; the cold reflection of silver circles in candlelight

### Lighting
- **Unnatural:** The light sources are wrong. Candles burn steadily without flickering. The fireplace gives light but little heat. Some jars glow on their own. Shadows fall in directions that don't match the light sources.
- **Cold:** The quality of light is cold -- blue-white, green-tinged -- rather than the warm amber of normal firelight
- **Dark corners:** Despite the light, certain corners seem to resist illumination, remaining stubbornly dark

### Atmosphere/Smell Cues
- **Death:** The sweet, cloying smell of decay -- not fresh rot, but the preserved, embalmed scent of a mortuary. Formaldehyde-like chemical preservatives (alchemical, since formaldehyde doesn't exist yet).
- **Incense:** Something burned to mask the death-smell -- a heavy, bitter incense (myrrh, wormwood, yew bark)
- **Metal:** Cold iron and tarnished silver
- **Ozone:** A metallic, electric tang from residual magical energy
- **Old books:** Dust, parchment, old leather
- **Cold:** The tower is always cold, regardless of the season. Breath sometimes fogs even in summer.
- **Sound:** Silence, broken by: the turning of pages, the clink of glass, a distant whispering that might be wind or might be voices, the scratch of a quill, the occasional groan from something in the cellar (yes, there's a cellar -- flooded, dark, and containing things the necromancer has forgotten about or wishes to forget)
- **Wrongness:** An ineffable sense that reality is thin here. The air pressure changes without cause. Time seems to move differently.

## 7.3 Corrupted Chapel

### Purpose
A once-holy building that has been desecrated and claimed by dark forces. This may have happened through: a massacre within its walls, a priest who turned to dark worship, a demonic incursion, or the creeping influence of a cursed artifact hidden within. The corruption is both spiritual and physical -- the building itself is changing.

### Exterior Appearance
- **Original structure:** A standard village chapel (see 3.1), but twisted. The stone has darkened beyond weathering -- a stained, sickly grey-brown, as if bruised. The roofline sags. The bell in the bellcote is cracked or missing.
- **Vegetation:** The churchyard is overgrown, but the growth is wrong -- thorny brambles with no leaves, black ivy that seems to move, pale mushrooms growing in profusion. The yew tree is dead, its bark peeling to reveal black wood beneath.
- **The door:** Swollen and warped, difficult to open. The iron fittings are deeply corroded with rust that looks like dried blood. The protective symbols carved above the door have been scratched out or inverted.
- **Windows:** The glass (if any) is cracked or shattered. From certain angles, a faint reddish or greenish light is visible inside, even when no candles are lit.
- **Smell (from outside):** A sick-sweet smell of rot and something chemical or metallic -- wrong for a church

### Interior

**Nave:**
- **The Doom painting** above the chancel arch is unchanged but now seems prescient rather than cautionary -- the demons look triumphant
- **Wall paintings:** The existing murals have... changed. Saints' faces are contorted in pain. The Virgin's eyes follow you. A new figure has appeared in the Last Judgment that wasn't there before -- a shadow with too many limbs.
- **Floor:** The flagstones are cracked, and from the cracks grows a fine, pale moss or a dark, root-like growth that pulses faintly. In the center of the nave, a dark stain spreads -- it looks like a bloodstain but is slightly raised, almost organic.
- **Font:** The holy water has turned black and viscous. The stone font is cracked. Something lives in the water.
- **Rood screen:** The painted crucifix has rotted -- the wooden Christ figure's face has warped into an expression of despair or rage. The paint has run in places like tears of blood.
- **Pews/benches:** Overturned, broken, or arranged in a circle around a central point (a makeshift ritual space)
- **Candles:** There are candles -- dozens of them, in places no one lit them. Black tallow candles, burned down to various heights, the wax pooling in organic patterns on the floor. Some are still flickering.
- **Altar desecration:** The altar cloth is stained and torn. The cross is inverted or replaced with an idol -- a crude figure of stone or bone. Dark offerings (dead animals, strange fetishes) are arranged on the altar.
- **Graffiti:** Occult symbols scratched or painted on the walls in what might be blood -- pentagrams, sigils, writing in an unknown script
- **Sound:** Even in silence, there's a sound -- a low, subsonic hum, or a whisper just below the threshold of hearing. The acoustics of the building have changed: your footsteps echo wrong.

**Chancel:**
- The most corrupted area
- The altar is now a focus of dark power -- touching it causes nausea or visions
- The piscina (the stone drain) is seeping a dark, oily liquid
- The east window's glass shows a different image from outside and inside -- from inside, the saint depicted seems to be screaming

**Crypt (if present):**
- The tombs are disturbed -- lids shifted, contents partially visible
- Scratch marks on the inside of stone lids
- The air is thick and wrong
- Something moves in the deepest part of the crypt

### Textures and Colors
- **Corruption:** Dark grey stone stained with blacks, browns, and sickly yellows. Organic growths (moss, fungus, root-tendrils) in pale white, fleshy pink, and necrotic black.
- **Blood:** Dried brown-black on the floor and altar, fresh-looking red in the graffiti
- **Candle wax:** Black, with a slight iridescence
- **Metal:** Everything corroded -- iron to deep rust, brass to verdigris, silver to black tarnish
- **Wood:** Rotting, grey, soft to the touch in places. The rood screen's paint is blistered and peeling.
- **The overall palette has shifted:** Where a chapel should be cool grey stone and warm candlelight, this is dark, warm (blood-warm), and tainted with greens and reds that shouldn't be there

### Lighting
- **Unnatural:** The chapel is darker than it should be, even at noon. Shadows pool in corners and don't move when you bring a light closer.
- **The candles:** Provide a flickering, uncertain light. The flames are slightly too tall and too yellow-orange, almost red at the base.
- **From the cracks:** A faint luminescence from the growths in the floor cracks -- pale green or pale red
- **Window light:** Dirty, filtered, with a reddish cast

### Atmosphere/Smell Cues
- **Rot:** The sweet, heavy smell of organic decay -- not clean forest-floor decomposition, but something sick and warm
- **Iron/blood:** A metallic tang, like holding an old coin in your mouth
- **Extinguished candles:** The acrid smell of snuffed wicks
- **Incense -- but wrong:** As if the frankincense has been mixed with something foul. Bitter, cloying, with an undertone of sulfur.
- **Cold pockets:** The overall temperature may be slightly warm (the corruption generates heat), but there are spots of intense, localized cold -- places where something unseen lingers
- **Emotional atmosphere:** Dread. An instinctive, animal revulsion. The hindbrain screaming to leave. This is a place where something terrible happened, and the echo hasn't faded -- it's grown stronger.

## 7.4 Abandoned Mine Entrance

### Purpose
The mouth of a played-out, collapsed, or cursed mine. Medieval mines extract iron, tin, copper, lead, silver, gold, or coal. This mine was abandoned -- perhaps the ore ran out, perhaps miners broke into something they shouldn't have, perhaps too many workers died in cave-ins or from "bad air." Now it's a dungeon entrance, a monster lair, or a passage to the underworld.

### Exterior Appearance
- **Location:** A hillside or cliff face, often remote. An old mining road (now overgrown) leads to it from the nearest settlement.
- **Entrance:** A dark opening in the rock face, roughly 6-8 feet wide and 5-6 feet high. Originally shored up with heavy timber frames (the headframe), now partially collapsed. Rotting timber posts lean inward, held together by rusted iron bolts and straps.
- **Spoil heap:** A large pile of waste rock (tailings) spreads downhill from the entrance -- grey, reddish, or dark stone fragments, mixed with soil. Sparse, sickly vegetation grows on it.
- **Equipment remnants:** A rusted iron ore cart on corroded rails, its wheels seized. Rotting wooden wheelbarrows. A broken windlass frame over a vertical shaft (if the mine had one). Coils of frayed rope. Discarded tools: a rusted pick head, a broken shovel.
- **Warning signs:** Locals have tied warning markers (red cloth strips) to stakes near the entrance. Perhaps a crude sign or a holy symbol nailed to the headframe.
- **Natural overgrowth:** Brambles, ivy, and ferns partially conceal the entrance. Bats roost in the overhang.
- **Water:** A slow trickle of mineral-stained water (orange-red from iron, or milky-white from calcium) seeps from the mine entrance, staining the rocks below.

### Interior

**Entrance Tunnel:**
- Roughly hewn from rock, 6-8 feet wide, 5-6 feet high (or lower -- mining tunnels were often cramped)
- Timber shoring: vertical posts and horizontal planks (some rotten, some collapsed, revealing raw rock)
- Floor: rough rock with standing water in low spots, a channel where an old rail track runs (the iron rails are corroded)
- Walls: bare rock showing the seams and colors of the geology -- grey limestone, dark shale, rust-red ironstone, occasionally a glint of the mineral that was mined
- Timber props are wrapped with fungus -- white, translucent cave mushrooms and dark slime molds
- Tools left behind: a pick driven into the wall and left, a candle niche cut into the rock with a puddle of ancient wax, a tin lunch pail rusted through

**First Chamber:**
- A widened area where miners gathered or sorted ore
- A rusted iron ore cart, partially filled with rock
- Shelving or niches in the wall for candles and supplies
- A collapsed timber frame partially blocking a side passage
- A vertical shaft (a "stope" or "winze") descending into darkness, with a rotten rope dangling into it
- Graffiti from miners: initials, dates, crude religious symbols (crosses, prayers for protection)
- Dead air pockets: certain passages have no air movement -- a candle flame stands perfectly still, then gutters

**Deeper Passages:**
- The tunnels narrow and branch
- Some passages are flooded (black, still water of unknown depth)
- Some end in cave-ins (a wall of broken rock and timber)
- Others continue into natural cave systems where the miners broke through
- Evidence of the cause of abandonment:
  - Claw marks on the walls (something dug from the other side)
  - A miner's skeleton, still in its rotting leather jerkin, slumped against the wall
  - A vein of ore that changes color from natural iron-red to an unnatural purple-black
  - A draft from deep below, carrying a smell that is not geological

### Textures and Colors
- **Rock:** Every shade of grey, brown, and rust-red, with occasional metallic glints (pyrite, galena, native copper)
- **Timber:** Rotting -- grey, soft, fibrous, with white and orange fungus
- **Metal:** Deep rust-red and rust-brown on every iron surface
- **Water:** Iron-orange, calcium-white, or clear-but-dark
- **Fungus:** White, pale yellow, translucent grey, and (deeper in) faintly luminescent blue-green
- **Overall:** Dark. Overwhelmingly dark. Color only exists where you bring light.

### Lighting
- **None:** Total darkness beyond 10 feet from the entrance.
- **Brought light:** A torch or lantern creates a sphere of warm orange light surrounded by absolute black. Shadows are sharp-edged and total. The rock walls absorb light.
- **Deep mine:** Faint bioluminescence from cave organisms (mushrooms, algae) provides an eerie, pale blue-green glow -- enough to see shapes but not details.

### Atmosphere/Smell Cues
- **Mineral:** The cold, sharp smell of exposed rock -- flint-like, slightly metallic
- **Damp:** Constant moisture, dripping water, puddles
- **Rot:** Decaying timber -- a wet, fungal smell
- **Bad air:** In dead-end passages, the air is thin and stale. Carbon dioxide pools in low areas. The candle flame burns dim and yellow, or goes out entirely (a traditional miner's warning).
- **Metal:** Rust, iron, and in some mines, the acrid smell of sulfur or arsenic (from mineral deposits)
- **Deep mine:** Something else -- a warm, organic smell rising from below that doesn't belong underground. Ozone. The faintest hint of rot, or of something alive and very large.
- **Sound:** Dripping water (ubiquitous), the creak of settling timber, the distant rumble of unstable rock, your own breathing amplified by the silence, and -- if you're still long enough -- a sound from deep below that might be rushing water or might be breathing

## 7.5 Seer's Observatory

### Purpose
Home and workspace of a seer, astrologer, or diviner -- one who reads the stars, interprets omens, and perceives the threads of fate. Unlike the witch (who works with earthy folk magic) or the necromancer (who traffics with death), the seer is a scholar of cosmic patterns and prophecy. In a dark fantasy setting, the seer has glimpsed too much -- their mind is frayed, their eyes have changed, and their predictions carry a terrible accuracy.

### Exterior Appearance
- **Structure:** A tall, narrow tower or the upper floors of a converted building. The defining feature is a rooftop observatory -- an open platform or a room with a retractable or removable roof section (wooden panels on runners). The tower rises above surrounding buildings for a clear view of the sky.
- **Materials:** Stone or half-timber, weathered and slightly unkempt. Astronomical symbols (zodiac signs, planetary glyphs) may be carved into the stonework or painted on the plaster.
- **Roof:** A domed or flat top with the observation platform. An iron weather vane or an armillary sphere silhouette visible from below.
- **Windows:** Unusual for the period -- larger than normal, positioned to observe specific sky quadrants. One window may be covered with a translucent crystal or colored glass.
- **Condition:** The seer is not concerned with housekeeping -- the exterior is neglected, with peeling plaster and overgrown ivy.

### Interior

**Ground Floor -- Consultation Room:**
- Where the seer receives visitors seeking prophecy
- A small, dim room with heavy curtains over the windows
- A round table covered with a dark cloth (midnight blue or black with embroidered silver stars)
- A crystal ball (polished quartz sphere) on a stand at the center
- A tarot deck or set of divination cards spread on the table
- A comfortable chair for the client; the seer's chair is taller, with arm rests
- Incense burners (sandalwood, frankincense) -- smoke creates a haze
- Wall hangings depicting celestial maps or zodiac wheels
- A small payment box (the seer charges for readings)

**Middle Floor -- Study:**
- The seer's working library and calculation space
- A large desk covered with:
  - Star charts and celestial maps (hand-drawn on parchment, incredibly detailed)
  - Astronomical tables (numerical data for predicting planetary positions)
  - An astrolabe (a brass instrument for measuring star positions -- the most prized possession)
  - A quadrant (a quarter-circle of brass for measuring angles)
  - An orrery (a mechanical model of the solar system -- rare and fantastical)
  - Compasses, rulers, and dividers for geometric calculations
  - Stacks of parchment with calculations, predictions, and natal charts for clients
- Bookshelves with astronomical treatises, Ptolemaic texts, Arabic star catalogues, and prophetic works
- A globe (terrestrial or celestial) -- if early enough, a flat map with fanciful edges
- A sand glass (hourglass) and a clepsydra (water clock) for precise timekeeping

**Top Floor -- The Observatory:**
- A room with a section of roof that can be opened to the sky (wooden panels on rollers, or a retractable canvas cover)
- **The telescope (if post-1600) or:** The seer uses naked-eye observation aided by:
  - A large armillary sphere (metal rings representing celestial circles, large enough to sight through)
  - Sighting tubes (long brass tubes without lenses that help focus the eye on a specific point)
  - A cross-staff or Jacob's staff for measuring angular distances
- A rotating platform or chair that allows the observer to turn smoothly to different sky positions
- A flat writing surface for recording observations in real-time
- A sky-mapping board: a round board with a grid on which the seer plots star positions during observation sessions
- No fire -- the observatory must be dark for star-watching. A single red-filtered candle (light through a red crystal) for reading without losing night vision
- Heavy blankets and a fur cloak (observing is cold work)

**Dark Fantasy Additions:**
- One corner of the study has a mirror that shows a different sky than the one outside
- The star charts include constellations that don't exist -- the seer maps a sky that only they can see
- Stains on the floor of the observatory in the shape of a ritual circle -- the seer has tried to contact something above
- A jar containing a small, glowing object -- a fallen star, a bottled moonbeam, or a crystallized omen
- The seer's eyes: milky white, or with pupils that reflect constellations. They see too much and have difficulty distinguishing past, present, and future
- Written prophecies pinned to the walls, some in the seer's hand, some in a hand they don't recognize

### Textures and Colors
- **Dominant:** Midnight blue, silver, brass, dark wood, cream parchment
- **The observatory at night:** Black sky dotted with silver stars, the brass instruments catching faint light
- **The consultation room:** Dark and mysterious -- deep blue hangings, silver thread, crystal refractions
- **The study:** A scholar's clutter -- warm parchment tones, dark ink, the golden gleam of brass instruments

### Lighting
- **Consultation room:** Deliberately dim and atmospheric -- incense smoke, a few candles, the glow of the crystal ball
- **Study:** Good natural light from the large windows during the day; candles and an oil lamp at night
- **Observatory:** Dark. Deliberately dark. Only the stars provide light. A red-filtered candle for note-taking.

### Atmosphere/Smell Cues
- Sandalwood and frankincense incense (consultation room)
- Old parchment, ink, and brass polish (study)
- Cold night air and the fresh, clean scent of open sky (observatory)
- A faint ozone or electric tang (the residue of divination -- the seer's magic smells like a lightning storm about to break)
- The sense that time is not quite right here -- clocks run fast or slow, the shadows don't match the sun's position
- Dark fantasy: a growing unease. The seer has seen the end of everything. It's written in the stars, and the stars are watching back.

## 7.6 Plague Doctor's House

### Purpose
Home and workshop of a plague doctor -- a physician specializing in treating victims of epidemic disease (the Black Death and its recurrences). The plague doctor is both healer and harbinger of death; their arrival in a town means the plague has come. Their house serves as a dispensary, quarantine assessment point, and research facility. In a dark fantasy setting, the plague may be supernatural in origin, and the doctor's methods blur the line between medicine and dark arts.

### Exterior Appearance
- **Location:** At the edge of town, near the plague pits (mass burial grounds) or the lazar house (plague hospital). Deliberately isolated.
- **Structure:** A narrow stone townhouse, 2 stories, with the windows partially boarded or covered with oiled cloth. The door has a large painted red cross or plague symbol.
- **Fumigation:** A small iron brazier by the door burning aromatic herbs (juniper, rosemary, pitch) to "purify" the air. Bundles of dried herbs hang around the doorframe.
- **Warning signs:** Chalk marks on the road indicating plague houses nearby. A bell mounted by the door that visitors must ring (the doctor comes to the door -- you do not enter uninvited).
- **Distinctive features:** A birdlike beak mask hung on a hook by the door (the iconic plague doctor mask -- a leather mask with a long beak filled with aromatic herbs, glass eye-holes, and a leather hood). A long waxed-leather coat on a peg. A wide-brimmed hat.

### Interior Rooms

**Ground Floor -- Consultation/Treatment Room:**
- The front room where the doctor sees patients (at arm's length, through a barrier if possible)
- A heavy oiled-leather curtain divides the room -- the doctor on one side, the patient on the other
- **Doctor's side:**
  - A writing desk with case records, ink, quill -- the doctor logs every case, every death, every symptom
  - A shelf of reference books (medical texts, herbal remedies, and in dark fantasy, texts on supernatural plagues)
  - A large brass incense burner, perpetually smoking with juniper, rosemary, and camphor
  - The plague doctor's equipment hung on the wall:
    - The beak mask (leather, with glass eye-lenses sealed with wax)
    - A long wooden pointer (for examining patients without touching them)
    - Leather gloves soaked in wax
    - The full-length waxed-leather coat
    - A wide-brimmed leather hat
  - A medicine chest: a portable wooden box with compartments containing:
    - Theriac (a compound medicine, the medieval panacea -- a paste of dozens of ingredients including opium, viper flesh, and spices)
    - Rose water
    - Vinegar (for cleaning and dousing cloths held over the face)
    - Camphor crystals
    - Mercury compounds (toxic but believed curative)
    - Laudanum (opium tincture -- for pain relief)
    - Leeches in a jar
    - A lancet and cupping glasses (for bloodletting)
    - Plasters and poultices
- **Patient's side:**
  - A simple wooden chair
  - A chamber pot
  - A bucket for vomit or blood
  - A straw palliasse on the floor (for collapsed patients)
  - Lime powder in a bucket (for disinfection)

**Ground Floor -- Preparation Room (Rear):**
- A worktable for preparing medicines
- A mortar and pestle (brass, stone)
- A small distillation apparatus for preparing tinctures and aromatic waters
- Bundles of dried herbs: lavender, rosemary, thyme, wormwood, sage, rue, angelica root
- Jars of mineral compounds: sulfur, arsite, mercury, camphor
- A pot of vinegar for soaking cloths
- Waxed-leather aprons hanging on hooks
- A fire for heating preparations and sterilizing tools
- A bucket of lime for cleaning

**Upper Floor -- Living Quarters:**
- Spartan -- the plague doctor lives alone (family sent away or dead)
- A narrow bed with linen sheets, regularly washed in vinegar
- A personal collection of books (medical, alchemical, and in dark fantasy, forbidden knowledge about the plague's true nature)
- A writing desk where the doctor works late, recording observations and searching for a cure
- A locked cabinet containing:
  - Patient records
  - Death counts by district (mapped on a hand-drawn city plan pinned to the wall)
  - Notes on the plague's progression, incubation period, and patterns
  - In dark fantasy: evidence that the plague is not natural -- tissue samples that glow, blood that moves on its own, drawings of organisms visible only through a special lens
- A personal fumigation setup: incense burner, aromatic herbs, a pot of burning pitch
- A crucifix on the wall (the doctor prays, though their faith is tested)
- A mirror (the doctor checks themselves for symptoms daily)

### Textures and Colors
- **Dominant:** Clinical, harsh -- white lime, dark waxed leather, amber glass, the yellow of sulfur, the dark brown of dried herbs
- **Leather:** The waxed coat and mask are distinctive -- dark brown-to-black, with a waxy sheen that makes them water-resistant
- **Glass:** Amber bottles, green-tinted phials, the round glass eye-pieces of the mask
- **The plague's colors:** Sickly yellow-green (infected tissue), dark purple-black (necrotic flesh, buboes), bright red (blood), white (lime powder, chalk quarantine marks)
- **Smoke:** The room is always hazy with aromatic smoke, giving everything a soft, slightly obscured quality

### Lighting
- **Consultation room:** Dim -- windows covered to prevent "bad air" from entering. The incense smoke filters what light there is.
- **Preparation room:** Better lit for precise work -- a window and candles
- **Upper floor:** Moderate daylight through partially uncovered windows

### Atmosphere/Smell Cues
- **Aromatic herbs and smoke:** The overwhelming sensory experience. Juniper, rosemary, camphor, and burning pitch create a thick, resinous, medicinal miasma -- deliberately strong to mask the smell of disease and to (in contemporary belief) ward off the plague.
- **Vinegar:** Sharp, acetic acid tang from soaked cloths and cleaning
- **Sulfur:** A sharp, matches-like chemical smell from fumigation
- **Underneath all the masking scents:** The sweet, terrible smell of plague -- rotting flesh, infected wounds, suppurating buboes. The doctor can never fully escape it.
- **Waxed leather:** The coat and mask have their own smell -- leather, beeswax, and the herbs stuffed in the beak
- **Dark fantasy:** A wrongness beneath the disease. The plague smells of something alive, intentional, and hungry. The doctor has noticed the plague seems to respond to the treatments -- not by retreating, but by adapting. The latest tissue samples show structures that look almost like eyes.
- **Emotional atmosphere:** Grim determination, loneliness, exhaustion. The plague doctor is among the bravest and most isolated figures in the medieval world. They walk toward death every day. Their house is a fortress against despair as much as disease.

## APPENDIX: UNIVERSAL MEDIEVAL INTERIOR ELEMENTS

These appear across multiple building types and should be included as appropriate:

### Common Fixtures
- **Rush lights:** Dried rush stalks dipped in tallow, held in iron clips -- the cheapest lighting
- **Tallow candles:** Made from rendered animal fat -- smoky, smelly, yellow light
- **Beeswax candles:** Cleaner, sweeter, brighter -- expensive, only for the wealthy or the Church
- **Braziers:** Iron or brass portable fire-pits for heating small rooms
- **Chamber pots:** Ceramic or pewter, under every bed
- **Privies (garderobes):** Stone shafts in castle walls, or wooden outhouses over pits
- **Straw/rush floor coverings:** Universal in all but the poorest and the church floors
- **Iron door hardware:** Strap hinges, ring handles, latches, bolts -- hand-forged, crude but strong
- **Wooden shutters:** Universal window covering; glass is a luxury
- **Holy items:** A crucifix or devotional image in almost every dwelling, from cottage to castle

### Common Sounds
- Bells (church bells marking the hours -- heard everywhere)
- Dogs barking, roosters crowing
- Cart wheels on cobblestones
- The crier announcing news
- Hammering from workshops
- The general buzz of human settlement
